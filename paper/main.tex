\begin{abstract}
State-of-the-art laws for predicting the LLM pretraining loss curve under a learning-rate
(LR) schedule---the Multi-Power Law (MPL) and the Functional Scaling Law (FSL)---are
\emph{adiabatic}: they predict the quasi-static loss a schedule would reach at equilibrium,
depending on the schedule only through its integrated history. We show this leaves a
systematic \emph{positive} residual on \emph{fast} (warmup-stable-decay) decays---a
non-adiabatic relaxation lag, the loss falling behind its equilibrium when the LR is annealed
faster than it can relax. Under weak stability assumptions we derive a linear-response law: the leading residual is a
causal convolution of per-step LR decrements with a relaxation kernel in cumulative-LR time.
The operational correction is its lowest-capacity one-pole closure; in a noise-dominated
AdamW specialization the amplitude is the equilibrium-floor sensitivity and the relaxation
time is the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$. On public LLM curves the
residual indeed relaxes with $\tau\propto1/\eta$ ($p=1.00\pm0.18$); since the kernel's rate
constant is empirically stable across the public scales, the correction can be transferred
with a small number of calibration constants, cutting held-out sharp-decay error by $44\%$
with no target-curve fitting. An independent reproduction---a separately-trained $\sim$10M transformer
and a from-scratch AdamW simulation---confirms the effect (the fast sweep ends higher
\emph{despite} $37\%$ more cumulative LR) and the amplitude identity (within $3\%$). The
correction complements MPL's exponent $\gamma$; its gain is in the data-poor, leading-order
regime.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The learning-rate schedule shapes the \emph{entire} loss trajectory of LLM
pretraining~\cite{kaplan2020,hoffmann2022,hu2024minicpm}. \emph{Schedule-aware loss-curve
prediction} asks: given $\{\eta_t\}$, predict $L(t)$, so that schedules can be compared and
optimized \emph{before} spending compute. The defining test is cross-schedule
generalization---fit on cosine schedules, predict the unseen WSD family. The state of the art
is the \textbf{Multi-Power Law} (MPL)~\cite{luo2025}, with a matching kernel-regression
theory, the \textbf{Functional Scaling Law} (FSL)~\cite{li2025fsl}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mpl}
L(t)=L_0+A\,S(t)^{-\alpha}+B\!\sum_{k\le t}\!\Delta\eta_k\,
G\!\big(\eta_k^{-\gamma}(S(t)-S(k))\big),\quad G(x)=1-(1+Cx)^{-\beta},
\end{equation}
where $S(t)=\sum_{i\le t}\eta_i$ and $\Delta\eta_k=\eta_{k-1}-\eta_k$.

\paragraph{The gap: existing laws are adiabatic.}
These laws \emph{do} use the LR decrements: MPL convolves $\Delta\eta_k$ with a saturating
kernel $G$, and its factor $\eta_k^{-\gamma}$ is a rate-of-decay knob. But this single
empirical exponent is their \emph{only} handle on how \emph{fast} the LR changes.
Structurally they predict the \emph{quasi-static} loss---the value the schedule would reach
if the loss stayed at equilibrium; the river-valley analysis~\cite{wen2024} makes this
explicit, modelling the noise penalty as set \emph{instantaneously} by the current LR. We
call such laws \emph{adiabatic}. Calibrated on slow schedules, $\gamma$ does not extrapolate
to fast WSD decays, where the loss genuinely lags equilibrium---leaving a structured
residual (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}) that no single rate exponent captures across schedules. Our
correction is a derived transient \emph{complementary} to $\gamma$, which tunes the adiabatic
kernel.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
\item We show that adiabatic loss-curve laws leave a systematic \emph{positive} residual on
fast decays, and identify it as a non-adiabatic relaxation lag (\S\ref{sec:resid}).
\item Under weak moving-equilibrium assumptions we derive a linear-response law for the
non-adiabatic residual, and use its lowest-capacity one-pole closure as the rate-dependent
term $\Drop$ (\S\ref{sec:theory}). A noise-dominated AdamW specialization identifies the
amplitude with $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ and gives the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$
relaxation time; our point is that this timescale governs the \emph{loss-curve-law residual}.
\item We confirm in LLM pretraining that the residual relaxes with $\tau\propto1/\eta$
($p=1.00\pm0.18$), self-consistently establishing its noise-dominated regime
(\S\ref{sec:exp}).
\item The kernel's rate constant is empirically stable on the public same-architecture
scales, enabling a low-calibration cross-scale transfer that improves held-out sharp-decay
loss by $44\%$ with no target-curve fitting (\S\ref{sec:exp}).
\item We give a fair-baseline diagnostic separating a genuine formula gain from an
information gap, clarifying exactly when the correction helps (\S\ref{sec:exp},\ref{sec:rel}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}\label{sec:rel}
\paragraph{Loss-curve laws.}
Tissue~et~al.~\cite{tissue2024}, MPL~\cite{luo2025}, and FSL~\cite{li2025fsl} predict the
trajectory from integrated-LR statistics (cumulative LR / intrinsic time / convolutional
functionals). FSL and its follow-ups also derive optimal schedules~\cite{li2025fsl}. They are adiabatic in our operational sense: after fitting the quasi-static schedule response,
they do not explicitly model the transient tracking error caused by a moving LR-dependent
noise floor. Our rate-dependent term is a low-capacity linear-response correction to that
residual and is complementary to MPL's exponent $\gamma$ (which tunes the adiabatic kernel),
not a replacement for it.

\paragraph{Cooldown dynamics and schedule geometry.}
The river-valley picture~\cite{wen2024} explains qualitatively why decay ``reveals
progress'', and explicitly models the noise penalty as \emph{instantaneous} in the current
LR---the adiabatic assumption we refine. Dremov~et~al.~\cite{dremov2025} study the cooldown
empirically (including the role of AdamW's $\beta_2$ and the decay shape), framing it through a
bias--variance trade-off rather than a rate-dependent lag. Late-decay
analyses~\cite{kaplan2020} and schedule-shape searches~\cite{naganuma2026} study related
questions (why a long high-LR phase helps; what optimal shapes look like) but do not model the
loss's rate dependence.

\paragraph{SGD relaxation physics.}
Our weak-assumption derivation follows the stochastic-approximation idea that local stability
of a fixed-LR system yields tracking of slowly moving equilibria~\cite{borkar2000,kushner2003};
constant-step SGD can also be viewed through an approximate stationary distribution~\cite{mandt2017}.
That a quadratic mode relaxes with $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$ and that the SGD noise floor
scales with $\eta$ are classical, from noisy-quadratic and Langevin analyses of
SGD~\cite{zhang2019nqm}; adaptive methods inherit a preconditioned version~\cite{malladi2022},
operating at an adaptive edge of stability~\cite{cohen2021,cohen2022}. We do not claim these
ingredients as new. Our contribution is the synthesis: identifying that adiabatic loss-curve
laws miss this moving-equilibrium relaxation lag, deriving the causal decrement-convolution
structure, and building a low-capacity cross-scale predictor from it.

\section{The non-adiabatic residual}\label{sec:resid}
We take a \emph{well-fit} MPL (the published parameters~\cite{luo2025}) and examine its
prediction residual $r(t)=L_{\mathrm{true}}(t)-L_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t)$ on held-out schedules.
(Isolating the non-adiabatic term requires a well-fit MPL: a baseline that already captures
the \emph{adiabatic} part cleanly, so the remainder is the lag and not a backbone mis-fit; a
cosine-only fit conflates the two and gives a far noisier residual.) The residual has a sharp
signature (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}): it is $\approx 0$ throughout the long stable phase and on
gradual cosine decay, but jumps \emph{positive} exactly when the LR decays steeply, and is
largest---and \emph{undamped at the curve's end}---for the sharpest decays (\code{wsd},
\code{wsdld}). The end-of-curve lag is $+(6\text{--}15)\times10^{-3}$ on WSD/WSDLD versus
$\lesssim\!4\times10^{-3}$ on cosine. Crucially, a gradual cosine and a sharp WSD decay reach
the \emph{same} final LR, yet only the fast one lags: same destination, faster sweep, bigger
lag---the textbook signature of a rate-dependent (non-adiabatic) effect, and exactly the
dependence an adiabatic law cannot represent.

\section{Theory: a linear-response correction}\label{sec:theory}
The empirical signature in \S\ref{sec:resid} is a tracking failure: after a sharp LR drop,
the quasi-static noise floor decreases immediately, but the optimizer's excess-loss state
relaxes only over a finite time. We make this statement precise in two layers. The main text
states the weak-assumption linear-response result and the low-capacity closure used for
prediction; the detailed proof is deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:linear_response}. AdamW is
used only as a mechanistic specialization that identifies the amplitude and the
$\tau\propto1/\eta$ timescale, not as a necessary assumption for the convolutional form.
This style of argument follows the standard stochastic-approximation view that constant- or
slowly-varying-step optimizers track stable local equilibria~\cite{borkar2000,kushner2003},
and is consistent with the stationary-distribution view of constant-step SGD~\cite{mandt2017}.

\paragraph{Adiabatic component and hidden excess-loss state.}
Let
\[
S_t=\sum_{u\le t}\eta_u
\]
be cumulative LR. We decompose the loss as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss_decomp_main}
L_t=L_{\mathrm{ad}}(S_t,\eta_t)+r_t,
\end{equation}
where $L_{\mathrm{ad}}$ denotes the quasi-static component that would be obtained if the
LR-dependent excess-loss state were equilibrated at the current LR. In experiments, a well-fit
MPL is used as an empirical surrogate for $L_{\mathrm{ad}}$; hence
$L_t-L_{\mathrm{MPL},t}$ estimates $r_t$ up to the remaining MPL approximation error.

The weak modeling assumption is that the LR-dependent excess loss is controlled by a hidden
state $z_t$ obeying
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hidden_dyn_main}
z_{t+1}=\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}(z_t),
\end{equation}
and that, for fixed $(S,\eta)$ in the decay window, there is a locally stable equilibrium
$z^\star(S,\eta)$ satisfying
\[
z^\star(S,\eta)=\Phi_{S,\eta}\big(z^\star(S,\eta)\big).
\]
The scalar excess-loss observable is $F_t=\ell(z_t)$, with equilibrium value
\[
F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S,\eta)=\ell\big(z^\star(S,\eta)\big).
\]
This assumption is intentionally weaker than a quadratic-loss or diagonal-noise model: $z_t$
may be a covariance state, a preconditioned noise-floor state, or any locally stable state
whose equilibrium depends on the LR.

\paragraph{Assumptions used by the main result.}
The detailed proof in Appendix~\ref{app:linear_response} uses the following local conditions.
First, $\Phi_{S,\eta}$, $z^\star(S,\eta)$, and $\ell$ are twice differentiable in a small
neighborhood of the decay window. Second, the linearized dynamics
\[
T_t=D_z\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}\big(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)\big)
\]
is stable in the sense that products $T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}$ are uniformly bounded and
summable over the window. Third, we isolate the response forced by LR decrements; the response
to slow $S$-drift is treated as a nuisance drift term already largely captured by the
adiabatic backbone. Fourth, for the sign statement only, we assume a positive response cone:
$\partial_\eta z^\star$ lies in a cone preserved by $T_t$, and the loss observable is
nonnegative on this cone. This last condition is not needed for the convolutional form; it
only formalizes why LR decay produces a positive residual.

\paragraph{Proposition: causal LR-decrement response.}
Define the tracking error
\[
\delta_t=z_t-z^\star(S_t,\eta_t),
\qquad
\Delta\eta_t^+=(\eta_t-\eta_{t+1})_+ .
\]
Under the above local assumptions, the cooldown-specific residual has the first-order form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:main_response}
r_t
=
D\ell_t\big[T_{t-1}\cdots T_0\delta_0\big]
+
\sum_{k<t}K_{k,t}\Delta\eta_k^+
+\mathcal E_t,
\end{equation}
where $D\ell_t=D\ell_{z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)}$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Kt_main}
K_{k,t}
=
D\ell_t\!\left[
T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\,\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)
\right].
\end{equation}
The first term is an initial transient; it is small when the decay begins after a long stable
phase. The error $\mathcal E_t$ is second order in the perturbation size and contains the
neglected $S$-drift and MPL-surrogate error. A representative bound is given in
Appendix~\ref{app:linear_response}; schematically,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:error_schematic_main}
|\mathcal E_t|
\lesssim
\sum_{k<t} q_{k,t}
\big(|\Delta\eta_k|^2+\|\delta_k\|^2+\|\epsilon^S_k\|+\eta_k^2\big)
+\epsilon_{\mathrm{MPL}},
\end{equation}
where $q_{k,t}$ is the stable propagation envelope and $\epsilon^S_k$ is the slow-geometry
forcing. Thus the leading non-adiabatic residual is not an arbitrary bump: it is the causal
linear response of a stable moving equilibrium to LR decrements.

\paragraph{Why the memory is in cumulative-LR time.}
To obtain the intrinsic-time kernel, add the standard first-order optimizer approximation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:T_first_order_main}
T_t=I-\eta_t A+O(\eta_t^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}),
\end{equation}
on the finite-dimensional active subspace that controls the excess loss, with bounded stable
operator $A$ \emph{treated as approximately constant over the decay window} (this is the
static-spectrum idealization; its slow variation is carried by $\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}$, and
constancy is precisely what turns the product into a genuine $S$-time exponential rather than a
time-ordered one). Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:product_exp_main}
T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}
=
\exp\!\left[-A(S_t-S_k)\right]
+
O\!\left(\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}\right).
\end{equation}
If the response of $A$ under the observable $D\ell$ admits a positive Laplace-mixture
representation (equivalently, $K$ is completely monotone---which holds when $A$ is symmetric
positive and the forcing $\partial_\eta z^\star$ aligns positively with $D\ell$, as for a
noise-floor relaxation), then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral_response_main}
r_t
\approx
\sum_{k<t}
\underbrace{\int e^{-a(S_t-S_k)}\,d\rho(a)}_{K(S_t-S_k)}
\Delta\eta_k^+ .
\end{equation}
This is the primitive scaling law: a fast-cooldown residual is a convolution of LR decrements
with a stable memory kernel in cumulative-LR time. Its instantaneous amplitude is fixed by
the equilibrium-floor LR sensitivity at fixed progress:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kernel_mass_main}
K(0)=
D\ell_{z^\star}\partial_\eta z^\star
=
\left.\partial_\eta F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S,\eta)\right|_S
=:\chi(S,\eta).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{One-pole closure and the operational law.}
The general kernel $K$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral_response_main} is not used as a flexible
fit. To avoid overfitting, we use the lowest-capacity stable closure: a single pole in
$S$-time, with a floor-sensitivity amplitude estimated from independent equilibrium probes.
Approximating $\chi(S,\eta)$ by a constant $\chi$ over the decay window gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:law}
\boxed{\;
L(t)=L_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t)
+c\,\chi\,
\underbrace{\sum_{k<t}e^{-\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}(S_t-S_k)}(\eta_k-\eta_{k+1})_+}_{\Drop_{\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}}(t)}
\;}
\end{equation}
where $c\le1$ accounts for compressing a spectral mixture into one effective pole and for
residual mismatch between MPL and the ideal adiabatic component. If the implementation defines
$\Drop$ using normalized drops $(\eta_k-\eta_{k+1})_+/\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$, then the prefactor
is equivalently $c\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\chi$; with raw LR drops, the dimensionally invariant
amplitude is $c\chi$.

\paragraph{AdamW specialization.}
The preceding response law does not rely on AdamW. AdamW supplies a local effective-mode
calculation that explains the observed amplitude and timescale. This reduction ignores
momentum transients, bias correction, decoupled weight decay, the numerical $\epsilon$, and
the finite-time dynamics of $v_t$; it should be read as a local noisy-quadratic approximation.
For a Hessian mode with curvature $\lambda_i$ and gradient-noise standard deviation $s_i$,
noise-dominated AdamW has $v_i^\star\approx s_i^2$, so
\[
x_{i,t+1}=\left(1-\eta_t\frac{\lambda_i}{s_i}\right)x_{i,t}-\eta_t\frac{1}{s_i}\xi_{i,t},
\qquad \mathbb E\xi_{i,t}^2=s_i^2.
\]
For $V_{i,t}=\mathbb E[x_{i,t}^2]$ and fixed LR $\eta$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Vi_rec_main}
V_{i,t+1}=\left(1-\eta\frac{\lambda_i}{s_i}\right)^2V_{i,t}+\eta^2,
\end{equation}
so
\[
V_i^\star(\eta)=
\frac{\eta^2}{1-(1-\eta\lambda_i/s_i)^2}
=\frac{\eta s_i}{2\lambda_i}+O(\eta^2).
\]
The modal excess-loss floor is
\[
F_i^\star(\eta)=\frac12\lambda_iV_i^\star(\eta)=\frac{\eta s_i}{4}+O(\eta^2),
\qquad
\frac{dF_i^\star}{d\eta}=\frac{s_i}{4}+O(\eta),
\]
and perturbations decay as
\[
\left(1-\eta\frac{\lambda_i}{s_i}\right)^2
=
\exp\!\left[-2\eta\frac{\lambda_i}{s_i}+O(\eta^2)\right].
\]
Thus the AdamW specialization of Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral_response_main} is the spectral mixture
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mix}
\Delta L(t)
\approx
\sum_i\frac{s_i}{4}
\sum_{k<t}e^{-2(\lambda_i/s_i)(S_t-S_k)}(\eta_k-\eta_{k+1})_+,
\qquad
\sum_i\frac{s_i}{4}
=
\frac{dF_{\mathrm{eq}}}{d\eta}+O(\eta).
\end{equation}
It predicts a positive lag, an amplitude given by the equilibrium-floor sensitivity, and a
step-time relaxation rate
\[
\tau_i^{-1}=2\eta\frac{\lambda_i}{s_i},
\qquad \tau_i\propto\eta^{-1}.
\]
The classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$ timescale is not itself new; our claim is that this
relaxation controls the residual left by adiabatic loss-curve laws after fast LR decay.

\paragraph{Predictions tested below.}
The theory predicts: (i) a positive residual after LR drops; (ii) a causal dependence on LR
\emph{decrements}, not merely on LR level; (iii) memory in cumulative-LR time; (iv) amplitude
controlled by the fixed-$S$ floor sensitivity $\chi$; (v) in the noise-dominated AdamW regime,
$\tau\propto1/\eta$; and (vi) because Eq.~\eqref{eq:mix} is a spectral mixture, a two-pole
kernel can improve diagnostics, while the main predictor deliberately uses the one-pole
closure Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} to avoid overfitting.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:exp}
\paragraph{Setup.}
All experiments use the public MPL loss curves (Llama-2-style $25/100/400$M, $9$ schedules
each)~\cite{luo2025}; a faithfully reproduced MPL (test MAE $0.0054/0.0050/0.0085$, matching
\cite{luo2025}) is the baseline throughout. We freeze the published MPL and compare it to
MPL$+$Eq.~\eqref{eq:law}; the only addition is the rate-dependent term. Unless noted
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{=}10$.

\paragraph{The rate prediction $\tau\propto1/\eta$ holds (Fig.~\ref{fig:tau}).}
The two-stage \code{wsdcon} curves step the LR down at step $8000$ to a constant
$\eta\!\in\!\{3,9,18\}\!\times\!10^{-5}$, giving a clean relaxation window. Fitting the
residual transient $r(t)\propto e^{-(t-8000)/\tau}$ yields $\tau$ that scales as $\eta^{-p}$
with \emph{pooled} $p=1.00\pm0.18$ (per scale $-1.06/-0.94/-0.51$ at $100/400/25$M). This is
the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$ confirmed for the loss-curve-law residual in LLM
pretraining, and---since $p{=}1$ is the noise-dominated value---it self-consistently justifies
the $v_i^*\!\approx\!s_i^2$ step of the derivation. The implied rate constant
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}=1/(\tau\eta)\approx10$ is approximately stable across the public
same-architecture scales; its modes have
$\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}\!\approx\!2\!\times\!10^{-3}\!\ll\!2$ (deep in the
slow regime, far from the edge of stability---unlike $\gamma$).

(A from-scratch AdamW simulation reproduces the same scaling, $p{=}0.97$; we defer it to the
independent reproduction in \S\ref{sec:repro2}, which covers it in more detail.)

\paragraph{The residual is the derived term.}
Regressing the well-fit MPL residual on $\Drop_{10}$ (through the origin) gives
$R^2{=}0.40/0.80/0.87$ at $25/100/400$M (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}); a 2-exponential kernel
improves $R^2$ by $0.06\text{--}0.07$, confirming the predicted spectral mixture
(Eq.~\ref{eq:mix}). The form is not arbitrary: in a fair few-shot comparison the derived
S-time exponential of decrements beats a step-time kernel ($-21\%$ vs.\ $-11\%$ held-out MAE)
and a memoryless floor ($-3\%$), confirming that the cumulative-LR ``time'' and the decaying
memory both matter (\code{repro/generic\_kernel\_compare.py}). (On a \emph{single} decay curve
the finite-$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ kernel is numerically close to the $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{\to}0$
adiabatic limit, so the rate constant is pinned only \emph{across} schedules; \S\ref{sec:repro2}.)

\paragraph{The amplitude is predictable from the noise floor.}
Estimating $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ \emph{independently} from the two-stage final losses
(noise floor vs.\ LR) predicts the fitted $\kappa$ up to a near-constant factor: the ratio
$\kappa_{\mathrm{fit}}/\kappa_{\mathrm{pred}}=0.43/0.51/0.60$ has coefficient of variation
$14\%$. With raw LR drops this gives $c\!\approx\!0.5$; if drops are normalised by
$\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ the same data require $c\,\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\approx0.5$,
i.e.\ $c\approx60$--$86$ (\S\ref{sec:repro2}). In either convention the near-constant ratio
is the transferable quantity (the $<\!1$ factor is the spectral spread of
Eq.~\ref{eq:mix}).

\paragraph{Low-calibration cross-scale transfer (Table~\ref{tab:xscale}).}
Because $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ and $c$ are empirically stable across these same-architecture
scales and $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ is read off the cheap noise-floor probes, the correction can
be transferred with \emph{zero fitting on the target curve}: take $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{=}10$, $c$ from a
leave-one-scale-out average of the \emph{other} scales, and $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ from the
target's own two-stage probes (distinct curves from the test). Applied to the held-out
\code{wsd}/\code{wsdld} decays it cuts MAE by $28\text{--}56\%$ ($44\%$ average, wins
$6/6$)---small-model constants plus a cheap probe predict the large-model sharp-decay curve
$\sim\!2\times$ better than MPL transfer.

\paragraph{Honest scope: when it does \emph{not} help.}
If instead one refits MPL on a few \emph{full} WSD curves, MPL itself absorbs the residual
(held-out MAE $0.0037\!\to\!0.0032$) and the explicit term adds nothing. So the residual is,
for a data-\emph{rich} MPL, an \emph{information} gap, not a formula gap: the value of the
correction is precisely the data-\emph{poor} regime---cosine plus cheap probes, but no full
WSD curves---which is the actual ``predict before training'' use case.

\paragraph{Relation to $\gamma$: complementary, not a substitute.}
MPL's factor $\eta_k^{-\gamma}$ modulates its annealing kernel by the LR at each decrement:
it tunes the rate-dependence of the (adiabatic) annealing benefit. Our term is the separate
(non-adiabatic) transient, and improves a \emph{full}-$\gamma$ MPL (Table~\ref{tab:xscale}) ---
the two are complementary. Characterising their precise relationship is left to future work
(\S\ref{sec:disc}).

\section{Independent reproduction}\label{sec:repro2}
An independent reproduction on different hardware (\code{represent/}; the authors' public
curves are no longer hosted, so it \emph{retrains} models) corroborates the mechanism along
two routes the public curves do not cover. All results below were subjected to an adversarial
audit (\code{results/AUDIT\_PARTC.md}): the auditor was tasked to \emph{falsify} each claim,
and only surviving readings are reported.

\paragraph{A real, independently-trained transformer (no fitting).}
A $\sim$10M-parameter Llama-2-style byte-level transformer was trained on WikiText with AdamW
($\beta_1{=}0.9$, $\beta_2{=}0.95$, $\mathrm{wd}{=}0.1$) under three schedules reaching the
\emph{same} final LR ($2.5{\times}10^{-3}{\to}2.5{\times}10^{-4}$) at different speeds
(Table~\ref{tab:real}). The fast (\code{wsd\_sharp}) sweep ends \emph{higher} than the gradual
ones \emph{despite spending $37\%$ more cumulative LR}---yet any law monotone in $S$ predicts
the opposite (more LR $\Rightarrow$ lower loss). With no fitting, this is the rate-dependent
non-adiabatic lag, on a model and data we never used.

A well-fit MPL (trained on \code{constant} plus two \code{wsdcon} curves, held-out MAE
$\approx0.005$) leaves residuals that sort by sweep speed: cosine $-0.011$ $<$ wsd-gradual
$-0.004$ $<$ \textbf{wsd-sharp $+0.006$} (the only positive). Across schedules, a
finite-$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ DropRelaxS kernel improves over the adiabatic
($\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{\to}0$) baseline by $\Delta R^2\!\approx\!+0.18$ on held-out data.
\emph{Note:} on a \emph{single} decay curve the finite-$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ kernel is
numerically degenerate with the adiabatic limit (corr$\approx0.99$); the rate dependence is
identifiable only \emph{across} schedules. The honest claim is therefore the no-fitting
Table~\ref{tab:real} signature plus the $+0.18$ joint advantage, not a single-curve $R^2$.

\paragraph{From-scratch AdamW confirms the mechanism quantitatively.}
Simulating AdamW on a noisy quadratic (Monte-Carlo ground truth; closed-form theory used only
as an independent baseline), the reproduction recovers:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
\item $\tau\propto1/\eta$ with $p=0.971$ (log-log $R^2{=}0.9996$), robust to leave-one-out and
  seed changes;
\item $\tau$ independent of $\beta_2\in[0.9,0.9999]$ (CV $1.1\%$), because the lag lives on
  noise-dominated modes where $v^*\approx s^2$ is constant;
\item the \textbf{amplitude identity} $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta=3.075$ versus the closed form
  $\tfrac14\sum_i s_i=3.0$ (within $3\%$);
\item a two-exponential kernel that recovers the injected preconditioned rates $2\lambda/s$
  ($\{1,16\}$ in the simulation, recovered as $\{0.87,16.1\}$).
\end{itemize}
The effect is falsifiable, not circular: a signal-dominated spectrum breaks $p{=}1$.

\paragraph{What does not transfer (consistent with our scope).}
At $\sim$10M the $\tau\propto1/\eta$ relation itself is \emph{not} resolved: the measured
post-step relaxation is roughly flat over a $16\times$ LR range ($\tau\approx475$--$912$
steps; slope $p\approx0.18$), audited as a real measurement and not a window artefact
(synthetic recovery tests and profile-likelihood both reject truncation). This matches the
paper's own statement that the signal grows strongly with scale; at notebook scale we confirm
the \emph{effect} but not its precise scaling law.

A second reproducible issue is a persistent $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ normalisation inconsistency:
if Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} is implemented with raw LR drops, the fitted $c$ is $O(1)$
($\approx0.5$); if normalized drops $(\eta_k-\eta_{k+1})_+/\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ are used, the
prefactor becomes $c\,\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\chi$ and the same data require $c\approx60$--$86$.
The paper's $c\approx0.5$ is therefore tied to a specific (unstated) normalisation convention.

Finally, several theory-driven extensions were tested in the controlled simulation
(\code{results/EXTENSIONS\_REPORT.md}). The cleanest success is a \textbf{spectral-mixture}
diagnostic: on a bimodal spectrum a two-exponential kernel improves $R^2$ by $0.065$ and
recovers the two theoretical rates $2\lambda/s$ (relative errors $0.13$ and $0.006$). A
cross-shape transfer of frozen parameters, however, \emph{fails} its pre-registered threshold
(mean $R^2{=}0.52$); and the open problem (a)---computing $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ and $c$
from first principles without an $O(1)$ fitted constant---remains open.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:disc}
The gain is regime-specific (data-poor sharp decays). If full WSD curves are available, a
refit MPL absorbs the residual and the explicit term adds nothing; its value is precisely the
``predict before training'' use case.

$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ and $c$ are \emph{measured}, not computed from first principles
(this needs the noise spectrum $\{s_i\}$, which we do not have). The controlled simulation
shows that $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ tracks the slowest preconditioned mode
$\lambda_i/s_i$ rather than a spectral average, but a robust predictor still requires an
unexplained $O(1)$ constant (open problem (a)).

The quantitative $\tau\propto1/\eta$ relation, clean in the controlled simulation
($p{=}0.971$), is \emph{not} resolved on the $\sim$10M transformer (\S\ref{sec:repro2}),
consistent with the paper's statement that the signal grows with scale. On that model the
finite-$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ kernel is numerically degenerate with the adiabatic baseline
on a single curve, so the rate dependence is identifiable only \emph{across} schedules.

There is a reproducible $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ normalisation ambiguity
(\S\ref{sec:repro2}): retaining the $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ factor in Eq.~\eqref{eq:law}
gives $c\approx60$--$86$, not the $c\approx0.5$ reported in the main experiments. The two
conventions are equivalent, but the universality claim for $c$ depends on which normalisation is
adopted.

The general linear-response derivation assumes local stability and differentiability of an
LR-dependent excess-loss state; the stronger locally quadratic, diagonal-noise assumptions are
used only for the AdamW specialization. Cross-shape transfer of frozen parameters fails its
pre-registered threshold, and the relationship between our correction and MPL's empirical
$\gamma$ remains qualitative. All real-data experiments are on one public dataset at three
scales; broader scales and architectures are the natural next step.

\section{Conclusion}
Existing loss-curve laws are adiabatic approximations. We identified and validated the leading
non-adiabatic correction: under weak stability assumptions it is a causal linear response to LR
decrements, and its one-pole closure gives a rate-dependent term whose relaxation time follows
the classical $\tau\propto1/\eta$ ($p=1.00\pm0.18$, confirmed for the loss-curve residual).
Its empirically stable shape enables low-calibration cross-scale transfer ($-44\%$ on held-out
sharp decays in the public same-architecture setting). An independent reproduction---a separately-trained $\sim$10M transformer and a
from-scratch AdamW simulation---confirms the effect (the fast sweep ends higher \emph{despite}
$37\%$ more cumulative LR) and the amplitude identity (within $3\%$), while also establishing
honest boundaries: the quantitative $\tau\propto1/\eta$ law is not resolved at $\sim$10M, the
kernel is degenerate on single curves, and an $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ normalisation ambiguity
remains. The loss curve depends not only on how much LR has been spent, but on how fast it is
being spent.

\section{Linear-response proof details}\label{app:linear_response}
This appendix gives the detailed derivation behind \S\ref{sec:theory}. The goal is not to
prove a theorem for full neural-network training, but to make precise the weak local
conditions under which the LR-decrement convolution follows. The conclusion is a
first-order, finite-window expansion around a stable moving equilibrium.

\paragraph{Formal local assumptions.}
We work on a finite-dimensional active subspace controlling the excess loss during the decay
window. Let $z_t$ obey $z_{t+1}=\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}(z_t)$ and let
$z^\star(S,\eta)$ be a fixed point. We assume:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
\item[(A1)] $\Phi_{S,\eta}$, $z^\star(S,\eta)$, and $\ell$ have bounded first and second
  derivatives in a tube of radius $R$ around the moving equilibrium.
\item[(A2)] The linearized products are stable: for $k<t$,
  $\|T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\|\le q_{k,t}$ with $\sum_{k<t}q_{k,t}\le Q$ on the finite window.
\item[(A3)] The LR decrement size $\Delta=\max_k|\eta_{k+1}-\eta_k|$ and the tracking error
  remain small enough that the Taylor expansions stay inside the tube.
\item[(A4)] The slow $S$-drift forcing is not the cooldown-specific object of interest. We
  denote it by $\epsilon^S_k$ and carry it in the error rather than absorbing it into the LR
  decrement term.
\item[(A5)] For the sign corollary only, there is a cone $\mathcal C$ such that
  $\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)\in\mathcal C$, $T_u\mathcal C\subseteq\mathcal C$, and
  $D\ell_t[v]\ge0$ for all $v\in\mathcal C$.
\end{itemize}
A1--A4 are enough for the convolutional response. A5 is only a clean sufficient condition for
positivity.

\paragraph{Tracking-error recursion.}
Define
\[
\delta_t=z_t-z^\star(S_t,\eta_t),
\qquad
T_t=D_z\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}\big(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)\big).
\]
By Taylor expansion and the fixed-point identity,
\begin{align}
 z_{t+1}
 &=\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}\big(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)+\delta_t\big) \\
 &=z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)+T_t\delta_t+R_t,
\end{align}
with $\|R_t\|\le C\|\delta_t\|^2$. Subtracting
$z^\star(S_{t+1},\eta_{t+1})$ gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app_delta0}
\delta_{t+1}=T_t\delta_t+
\big[z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)-z^\star(S_{t+1},\eta_{t+1})\big]+R_t.
\end{equation}
Expand the moving fixed point:
\begin{align}
z^\star(S_{t+1},\eta_{t+1})
&=z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)
  +\partial_Sz^\star(S_t,\eta_t)(S_{t+1}-S_t)
  +\partial_\eta z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)(\eta_{t+1}-\eta_t)
  +R_t^\star,
\end{align}
where $\|R_t^\star\|\le C(|S_{t+1}-S_t|^2+|\eta_{t+1}-\eta_t|^2)$. Since the first-order
$S$-drift is not the cooldown-specific LR-decrement forcing, define
\[
\epsilon_t^S=-\partial_Sz^\star(S_t,\eta_t)(S_{t+1}-S_t),
\]
after removing the component already modeled by the adiabatic backbone. Keeping the LR-drop
forcing gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app_delta_rec}
\delta_{t+1}=T_t\delta_t+
\partial_\eta z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)\Delta\eta_t^+ + e_t,
\end{equation}
where
\[
\|e_t\|\le C\big(\|\delta_t\|^2+|\Delta\eta_t|^2+\|\epsilon_t^S\|+\eta_t^2\big).
\]
The $\eta_t^2$ term is harmless on the finite window and accounts for the mismatch between
one-step discrete dynamics and the first-order continuous-time approximation used below.

\paragraph{Duhamel expansion and error.}
Iterating Eq.~\eqref{eq:app_delta_rec} yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app_duhamel}
\delta_t
=
T_{t-1}\cdots T_0\delta_0
+
\sum_{k<t}T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)\Delta\eta_k^+
+
\sum_{k<t}T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}e_k.
\end{equation}
Linearizing the observable gives
\[
r_t=F_t-F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S_t,\eta_t)
=D\ell_t[\delta_t]+O(\|\delta_t\|^2)+\epsilon_{\mathrm{MPL}},
\]
where $\epsilon_{\mathrm{MPL}}$ is present when MPL is used as a surrogate for the ideal
adiabatic component. Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:app_duhamel} gives Eq.~\eqref{eq:main_response}
with
\[
K_{k,t}=D\ell_t\!\left[T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)\right]
\]
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app_error_bound}
|\mathcal E_t|
\le
C\sum_{k<t}q_{k,t}
\big(\|\delta_k\|^2+|\Delta\eta_k|^2+\|\epsilon_k^S\|+\eta_k^2\big)
+C\|\delta_t\|^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{MPL}} .
\end{equation}
If the decay begins after a long stable phase, the initial transient
$D\ell_t[T_{t-1}\cdots T_0\delta_0]$ is exponentially or summably small under A2; otherwise it
is a separate transient and should not be attributed to the cooldown drop response.

\paragraph{Cumulative-LR time.}
Now add the first-order optimizer approximation
\[
T_u=I-\eta_uA+E_u,
\qquad
\|E_u\|\le C(\eta_u^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}),
\]
with bounded stable $A$. Since the factors $I-\eta_uA$ commute when $A$ is fixed,
\begin{align}
\prod_{u=k+1}^{t-1}(I-\eta_uA)
&=\exp\left(\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\log(I-\eta_uA)\right) \\
&=\exp\left(-A\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u+O\left(\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u^2\right)\right) \\
&=\exp\left(-A(S_t-S_k)\right)
  +O\left(\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u^2\right),
\end{align}
where the last step uses boundedness of $A$ on the active subspace. Including $E_u$ adds
$O(\sum_u\eta_u^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}})$, giving Eq.~\eqref{eq:product_exp_main}.
If the scalar response $D\ell_t[e^{-xA}\partial_\eta z^\star]$ is represented by a positive
Laplace mixture, then the kernel has the form in Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral_response_main}. The
single-pole predictor in Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} is the rank-one, lowest-capacity closure of this
general stable kernel.

\paragraph{Positivity.}
Under A5, $\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)\in\mathcal C$ and
$T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\mathcal C\subseteq\mathcal C$, so
$K_{k,t}\ge0$. If $\Delta\eta_k^+\ge0$, the leading response is positive. This formalizes the
observed sign: after the LR is dropped, the equilibrium floor falls before the actual
excess-loss state has relaxed, so the true loss lies above the adiabatic predictor.
