\begin{abstract}
State-of-the-art laws for predicting the LLM pretraining loss curve under a learning-rate
(LR) schedule---the Multi-Power Law (MPL) and the Functional Scaling Law (FSL)---are
\emph{adiabatic}: they predict the quasi-static loss a schedule would reach at equilibrium,
depending on the schedule only through its integrated history. We show this leaves a
systematic \emph{positive} residual on \emph{fast} (warmup-stable-decay) decays---a
non-adiabatic relaxation lag, the loss falling behind its equilibrium when the LR is annealed
faster than it can relax. Under weak stability assumptions we derive a linear-response law: the leading residual is a
causal convolution of per-step LR decrements with a relaxation kernel in cumulative-LR time.
The operational correction is its lowest-capacity one-pole closure; in a noise-dominated
AdamW specialization the amplitude is the equilibrium-floor sensitivity and the relaxation
time is the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$. Public LLM curves are consistent with this
mechanism ($p=1.00\pm0.18$), while our independently trained small transformer confirms the
lag but not a clean inverse-LR scaling. The kernel's rate constant is empirically stable
across the public scales, enabling a zero-target-fitting transfer that cuts held-out
sharp-decay error by $44\%$. We then upgrade the law's \emph{amplitude structure} in two
ways. First, a \emph{B-identity}: MPL's own annealing coefficient $B$ \emph{is} the
equilibrium-floor sensitivity the correction needs, so the amplitude transfers from MPL's
published parameters with no additional measurements ($-43\%$, matching the probe-based
chain). Second, the public-bed equilibrium floor reads \emph{superlinear} in $\eta$
($F_{\mathrm{eq}}\!\propto\!\eta^{1+\delta}$, $1{+}\delta=1.06$--$1.49$ from three-point
fits at $100/400$M)---though we show this reading is protocol-dependent (confound-free
equal-$S$ ladders on our own beds give a \emph{sublinear} exponent $\approx0.65$, robust
to model size, batch size, and horizon; \S\ref{sec:round2}), so the superlinearity is a
property of the settled-probe protocol, not established as a bed-independent law. The
corresponding second-order amplitude closure
$\chi(\eta)\!\propto\!\eta^{\delta}$ with default $\delta{=}1/4$ (Pareto-selected on the
audited matrix, never fit on targets) improves that transfer matrix on every aggregate and
lifts cheap-probe calibration by $35$--$70\%$ relative. An independent
reproduction---a separately-trained $\sim$10M transformer
and a from-scratch AdamW simulation---confirms the effect (the fast sweep ends higher
\emph{despite} $37\%$ more cumulative LR), the amplitude identity (within $3\%$), and,
on never-scanned out-of-family schedules, the second-order closure's
cheap-probe gains. The correction complements MPL's exponent $\gamma$; its gain is in
the data-poor, leading-order regime.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The learning-rate schedule shapes the \emph{entire} loss trajectory of LLM
pretraining~\cite{kaplan2020,hoffmann2022,hu2024minicpm}. \emph{Schedule-aware loss-curve
prediction} asks: given $\{\eta_t\}$, predict $L(t)$, so that schedules can be compared and
optimized \emph{before} spending compute. The defining test is cross-schedule
generalization---fit on cosine schedules, predict the unseen WSD family. The state of the art
is the \textbf{Multi-Power Law} (MPL)~\cite{luo2025}, with a matching kernel-regression
theory, the \textbf{Functional Scaling Law} (FSL)~\cite{li2025fsl}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mpl}
L(t)=L_0+A\,S(t)^{-\alpha}+B\!\sum_{k\le t}\!\Delta\eta_k\,
G\!\big(\eta_k^{-\gamma}(S(t)-S(k))\big),\quad G(x)=1-(1+Cx)^{-\beta},
\end{equation}
where $S(t)=\sum_{i\le t}\eta_i$ and $\Delta\eta_k=\eta_{k-1}-\eta_k$.

\paragraph{The gap: existing laws are adiabatic.}
These laws \emph{do} use the LR decrements: MPL convolves $\Delta\eta_k$ with a saturating
kernel $G$, and its factor $\eta_k^{-\gamma}$ is a rate-of-decay knob. But this single
empirical exponent is their \emph{only} handle on how \emph{fast} the LR changes.
Structurally they predict the \emph{quasi-static} loss---the value the schedule would reach
if the loss stayed at equilibrium; the river-valley analysis~\cite{wen2024} makes this
explicit, modelling the noise penalty as set \emph{instantaneously} by the current LR. We
call such laws \emph{adiabatic}. Calibrated on slow schedules, $\gamma$ does not extrapolate
to fast WSD decays, where the loss genuinely lags equilibrium---leaving a structured
residual (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}) that no single rate exponent captures across schedules. Our
correction is a derived transient \emph{complementary} to $\gamma$, which tunes the adiabatic
kernel.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
\item We show that adiabatic loss-curve laws leave a systematic \emph{positive} residual on
fast decays, and identify it as a non-adiabatic relaxation lag (\S\ref{sec:resid}).
\item Under weak moving-equilibrium assumptions we derive a linear-response law for the
non-adiabatic residual, and use its lowest-capacity one-pole closure as the rate-dependent
term $\Drop$ (\S\ref{sec:theory}). A noise-dominated AdamW specialization identifies the
amplitude with $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ and gives the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$
relaxation time; our point is that this timescale governs the \emph{loss-curve-law residual}.
\item Public LLM curves show residual relaxation consistent with $\tau\propto1/\eta$
($p=1.00\pm0.18$), while the separately trained $\sim$10M reproduction confirms the lag but
does not resolve this scaling; we therefore use inverse-LR relaxation as a mechanism check,
not as the main empirical claim (\S\ref{sec:exp}, \S\ref{sec:repro2}).
\item The kernel's rate constant is empirically stable on the public same-architecture
scales, enabling a low-calibration cross-scale transfer that improves held-out sharp-decay
prediction error (MAE) by $44\%$ with no target-curve fitting (\S\ref{sec:exp}).
\item We prove a \emph{B-identity}: MPL's fitted annealing coefficient $B$ equals the
MPL-implied equilibrium-floor sensitivity $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$, and verify it against
independent floor measurements ($5$--$15\%$ at all scales). The correction's amplitude then
transfers from MPL's own published parameters with no additional measurements ($-43\%$ on
the same protocol; \S\ref{sec:exp}).
\item The public-bed equilibrium floor reads \emph{superlinear} in $\eta$ at the two larger
scales (three-point fits, qualitative; marginal at $25$M); we use it to derive and
evaluate the corresponding second-order amplitude closure $\chi(\eta)\propto\eta^{\delta}$:
with the Pareto-selected default $\delta{=}1/4$ it improves the audited transfer matrix on
every aggregate (the hardest cell, cosine$\to$WSD, nearly doubles its gain) and
strengthens cheap-probe calibration by $35\%$ relative ($\sim\!65$--$70\%$ for the
in-sample $\delta{=}1/2$ variant); we also report exactly which
part of that gain is amplitude recalibration rather than shape (\S\ref{sec:exp}). The naive
NQM route to $\chi(\eta)$ is falsified at magnitude; a bulk$+$edge spectral mechanism is
consistent with all observations (\S\ref{sec:theory}).
\item A second pre-registered, adversarially-audited round (\S\ref{sec:round2}) sharpens
the scope: confound-free equal-$S$ floor ladders read \emph{sublinear} ($\approx0.65$,
robust across $10.7$/$25$M, batch size, and horizon to $24$k), so the public superlinearity
is shown to be settled-probe-\emph{protocol}-dependent rather than a parameter-count law;
the post-drop slow-mode clock is batch-size-\emph{blind} (not optimizer-noise relaxation);
and direct Hessian spectroscopy closes the spectral edge-re-pinning account three ways,
leaving the slow mode phenomenological on this bed.
\item We give a fair-baseline diagnostic separating a genuine formula gain from an
information gap, clarifying exactly when the correction helps, and close two structural
dead ends (multi-pole/Lomax kernel shapes; cross-family rate unification)
(\S\ref{sec:exp}, \S\ref{sec:rel}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}\label{sec:rel}
\paragraph{Loss-curve laws.}
Tissue~et~al.~\cite{tissue2024}, MPL~\cite{luo2025}, and FSL~\cite{li2025fsl} predict the
trajectory from integrated-LR statistics (cumulative LR / intrinsic time / convolutional
functionals). FSL and its follow-ups also derive optimal schedules~\cite{li2025fsl}. They are adiabatic in our operational sense: after fitting the quasi-static schedule response,
they do not explicitly model the transient tracking error caused by a moving LR-dependent
noise floor. Our rate-dependent term is a low-capacity linear-response correction to that
residual and is complementary to MPL's exponent $\gamma$ (which tunes the adiabatic kernel),
not a replacement for it.

\paragraph{Cooldown dynamics and schedule geometry.}
The river-valley picture~\cite{wen2024} explains qualitatively why decay ``reveals
progress'', and explicitly models the noise penalty as \emph{instantaneous} in the current
LR---the adiabatic assumption we refine. Dremov~et~al.~\cite{dremov2025} study the cooldown
empirically (including the role of AdamW's $\beta_2$ and the decay shape), framing it through a
bias--variance trade-off rather than a rate-dependent lag. Late-decay
analyses~\cite{kaplan2020} and schedule-shape searches~\cite{naganuma2026} study related
questions (why a long high-LR phase helps; what optimal shapes look like) but do not model the
loss's rate dependence.

\paragraph{SGD relaxation physics.}
Our weak-assumption derivation follows the stochastic-approximation idea that local stability
of a fixed-LR system yields tracking of slowly moving equilibria~\cite{borkar2000,kushner2003};
constant-step SGD can also be viewed through an approximate stationary distribution~\cite{mandt2017}.
That a quadratic mode relaxes with $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$ and that the SGD noise floor
scales with $\eta$ are classical, from noisy-quadratic and Langevin analyses of
SGD~\cite{zhang2019nqm}; adaptive methods inherit a preconditioned version~\cite{malladi2022},
operating at an adaptive edge of stability~\cite{cohen2021,cohen2022}. We do not claim these
ingredients as new. Our contribution is the synthesis: identifying that adiabatic loss-curve
laws miss this moving-equilibrium relaxation lag, deriving the causal decrement-convolution
structure, and building a low-capacity cross-scale predictor from it.

\section{The non-adiabatic residual}\label{sec:resid}
We take a \emph{well-fit} MPL (the published parameters~\cite{luo2025}) and examine its
prediction residual $r(t)=L_{\mathrm{true}}(t)-L_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t)$ on held-out schedules
(Fig.~\ref{fig:curve_overlay}).
(Isolating the non-adiabatic term requires a well-fit MPL: a baseline that already captures
the \emph{adiabatic} part cleanly, so the remainder is the lag and not a backbone mis-fit; a
cosine-only fit conflates the two and gives a far noisier residual.) The residual has a sharp
signature (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}): it is $\approx 0$ throughout the long stable phase and on
gradual cosine decay, but jumps \emph{positive} exactly when the LR decays steeply, and is
largest---and \emph{undamped at the curve's end}---for the sharpest decays (\code{wsd},
\code{wsdld}; the effect is visible at all three scales,
Fig.~\ref{fig:wsd_resid_scale}). The end-of-curve lag is $+(6\text{--}15)\times10^{-3}$ on WSD/WSDLD versus
$\lesssim\!4\times10^{-3}$ on cosine. Crucially, a gradual cosine and a sharp WSD decay reach
the \emph{same} final LR, yet only the fast one lags: same destination, faster sweep, bigger
lag---the textbook signature of a rate-dependent (non-adiabatic) effect, and exactly the
dependence an adiabatic law cannot represent.

\section{Theory: a linear-response correction}\label{sec:theory}
The empirical signature in \S\ref{sec:resid} is a tracking failure: after a sharp LR drop,
the quasi-static noise floor decreases immediately, but the optimizer's excess-loss state
relaxes only over a finite time. We make this statement precise in two layers. The main text
states the weak-assumption linear-response result and the low-capacity closure used for
prediction; the detailed proof is deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:linear_response}. AdamW is
used only as a mechanistic specialization that identifies the amplitude and the
$\tau\propto1/\eta$ timescale, not as a necessary assumption for the convolutional form.
This style of argument follows the standard stochastic-approximation view that constant- or
slowly-varying-step optimizers track stable local equilibria~\cite{borkar2000,kushner2003},
and is consistent with the stationary-distribution view of constant-step SGD~\cite{mandt2017}.

\paragraph{Adiabatic component and hidden excess-loss state.}
Let
\[
S_t=\sum_{u\le t}\eta_u
\]
be cumulative LR. We decompose the loss as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss_decomp_main}
L_t=L_{\mathrm{ad}}(S_t,\eta_t)+r_t,
\end{equation}
where $L_{\mathrm{ad}}$ denotes the quasi-static component that would be obtained if the
LR-dependent excess-loss state were equilibrated at the current LR. In experiments, a well-fit
MPL is used as an empirical surrogate for $L_{\mathrm{ad}}$; hence
$L_t-L_{\mathrm{MPL},t}$ estimates $r_t$ up to the remaining MPL approximation error.

The weak modeling assumption is that the LR-dependent excess loss is controlled by a hidden
state $z_t$ obeying
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hidden_dyn_main}
z_{t+1}=\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}(z_t),
\end{equation}
and that, for fixed $(S,\eta)$ in the decay window, there is a locally stable equilibrium
$z^\star(S,\eta)$ satisfying
\[
z^\star(S,\eta)=\Phi_{S,\eta}\big(z^\star(S,\eta)\big).
\]
The scalar excess-loss observable is $F_t=\ell(z_t)$, with equilibrium value
\[
F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S,\eta)=\ell\big(z^\star(S,\eta)\big).
\]
This assumption is intentionally weaker than a quadratic-loss or diagonal-noise model: $z_t$
may be a covariance state, a preconditioned noise-floor state, or any locally stable state
whose equilibrium depends on the LR.

\paragraph{Weak assumptions used by the main result.}
The detailed proof in Appendix~\ref{app:linear_response} uses only local conditions on the
decay window:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(A1)] \emph{Smooth moving equilibrium.} $\Phi_{S,\eta}$, $z^\star(S,\eta)$, and
$\ell$ are twice differentiable near the observed trajectory.
\item[(A2)] \emph{Local stability.} The linearized maps
$T_t=D_z\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t))$ have uniformly summable products:
$\|T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\|\le q_{k,t}$ with $\sup_t\sum_{k<t}q_{k,t}<\infty$.
\item[(A3)] \emph{Separated slow drift.} The first-order response to the gradual change of
$S_t$ is already included in the adiabatic backbone up to a small nuisance
$\epsilon^S_t$; we isolate the response forced by LR decrements.
\item[(A4)] \emph{First-order optimizer time.} On the active subspace,
$T_t=I-\eta_tA+E_t$ with stable bounded $A$ and
$\|E_t\|\le C(\eta_t^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}})$ over the cooldown window.
\item[(A5)] \emph{Positive response cone, only for the sign claim.}
$\partial_\eta z^\star$ lies in a cone preserved by $T_t$, and $D\ell$ is nonnegative on that
cone.
\end{itemize}
A1--A4 are enough to derive the current law's convolutional form; A5 is only a sufficient
condition for the positive residual sign.

\paragraph{Proposition: causal LR-decrement response.}
Define the tracking error
\[
\delta_t=z_t-z^\star(S_t,\eta_t),
\qquad
\Delta\eta_t^+=(\eta_t-\eta_{t+1})_+ .
\]
(Whether the per-decrement sensitivity is evaluated at the pre- or post-decrement LR is an
$O(\Delta\eta^2)$ choice within the first-order expansion; the implementation and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:law2} below use the post-decrement convention, matching
Eq.~\eqref{eq:mpl}.)
Under the above local assumptions, the cooldown-specific residual has the first-order form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:main_response}
r_t
=
D\ell_t\big[T_{t-1}\cdots T_0\delta_0\big]
+
\sum_{k<t}K_{k,t}\Delta\eta_k^+
+\mathcal E_t,
\end{equation}
where $D\ell_t=D\ell_{z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)}$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Kt_main}
K_{k,t}
=
D\ell_t\!\left[
T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\,\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)
\right].
\end{equation}
The first term is an initial transient; it is small when the decay begins after a long stable
phase. The error $\mathcal E_t$ is second order in the perturbation size and contains the
neglected $S$-drift and MPL-surrogate error. A representative bound is given in
Appendix~\ref{app:linear_response}; schematically,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:error_schematic_main}
|\mathcal E_t|
\lesssim
\sum_{k<t} q_{k,t}
\big(|\Delta\eta_k|^2+\|\delta_k\|^2+\|\epsilon^S_k\|+\eta_k^2\big)
+\epsilon_{\mathrm{MPL}},
\end{equation}
where $q_{k,t}$ is the stable propagation envelope and $\epsilon^S_k$ is the slow-geometry
forcing. Thus the leading non-adiabatic residual is not an arbitrary bump: it is the causal
linear response of a stable moving equilibrium to LR decrements.

\paragraph{Why the memory is in cumulative-LR time.}
To obtain the intrinsic-time kernel, add the standard first-order optimizer approximation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:T_first_order_main}
T_t=I-\eta_t A+O(\eta_t^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}),
\end{equation}
on the finite-dimensional active subspace that controls the excess loss, with bounded stable
operator $A$ \emph{treated as approximately constant over the decay window} (this is the
static-spectrum idealization; its slow variation is carried by $\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}$, and
constancy is precisely what turns the product into a genuine $S$-time exponential rather than a
time-ordered one). Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:product_exp_main}
T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}
=
\exp\!\left[-A(S_t-S_k)\right]
+
O\!\left(\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}\right).
\end{equation}
If the response of $A$ under the observable $D\ell$ admits a positive Laplace-mixture
representation (equivalently, $K$ is completely monotone---which holds when $A$ is symmetric
positive and the forcing $\partial_\eta z^\star$ aligns positively with $D\ell$, as for a
noise-floor relaxation), then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral_response_main}
r_t
\approx
\sum_{k<t}
\underbrace{\int e^{-a(S_t-S_k)}\,d\rho(a)}_{K(S_t-S_k)}
\Delta\eta_k^+ .
\end{equation}
This is the primitive scaling law: a fast-cooldown residual is a convolution of LR decrements
with a stable memory kernel in cumulative-LR time. Its instantaneous amplitude is fixed by
the equilibrium-floor LR sensitivity at fixed progress:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kernel_mass_main}
K(0)=
D\ell_{z^\star}\partial_\eta z^\star
=
\left.\partial_\eta F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S,\eta)\right|_S
=:\chi(S,\eta).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{One-pole closure and the operational law.}
The general kernel $K$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral_response_main} is not used as a flexible
fit. To avoid overfitting, we use the lowest-capacity stable closure: a single pole in
$S$-time, with a floor-sensitivity amplitude estimated from independent equilibrium probes.
Approximating $\chi(S,\eta)$ by a constant $\chi$ over the decay window gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:law}
\boxed{\;
L(t)=L_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t)
+c\,\chi\,
\underbrace{\sum_{k<t}e^{-\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}(S_t-S_k)}(\eta_{k-1}-\eta_k)_+}_{\Drop_{\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}}(t)}
\;}
\end{equation}
(LR-drop relaxation in $S$-time; the decrement into $\eta_k$ is deposited at $S_k$),
where $c\le1$ accounts for compressing a spectral mixture into one effective pole and for
residual mismatch between MPL and the ideal adiabatic component. If the implementation defines
$\Drop$ using normalized drops $(\eta_{k-1}-\eta_k)_+/\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$, then the prefactor
is equivalently $c\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\chi$; with raw LR drops, the dimensionally invariant
amplitude is $c\chi$.

\paragraph{Corollary: current law under the weak assumptions.}
Under A1--A4, if the initial transient at the decay start is negligible and the scalar
response kernel is dominated by one slow mode $a=\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$, then the leading
cooldown residual is exactly Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} up to the second-order remainder in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:error_schematic_main}. Thus the operational correction does not require a
quadratic network model: it is the one-pole closure of a stable moving-equilibrium response.
The AdamW calculation below is only used to identify $\chi$ and to motivate the expected
scale of $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$.

\paragraph{AdamW specialization.}
The preceding response law does not rely on AdamW. AdamW supplies a local effective-mode
calculation that explains the observed amplitude and timescale. This reduction ignores
momentum transients, bias correction, decoupled weight decay, the numerical $\epsilon$, and
the finite-time dynamics of $v_t$; it should be read as a local noisy-quadratic approximation.
For a Hessian mode with curvature $\lambda_i$ and gradient-noise standard deviation $s_i$,
noise-dominated AdamW has $v_i^\star\approx s_i^2$, so
\[
x_{i,t+1}=\left(1-\eta_t\frac{\lambda_i}{s_i}\right)x_{i,t}-\eta_t\frac{1}{s_i}\xi_{i,t},
\qquad \mathbb E\xi_{i,t}^2=s_i^2.
\]
For $V_{i,t}=\mathbb E[x_{i,t}^2]$ and fixed LR $\eta$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Vi_rec_main}
V_{i,t+1}=\left(1-\eta\frac{\lambda_i}{s_i}\right)^2V_{i,t}+\eta^2,
\end{equation}
so
\[
V_i^\star(\eta)=
\frac{\eta^2}{1-(1-\eta\lambda_i/s_i)^2}
=\frac{\eta s_i}{2\lambda_i}+O(\eta^2).
\]
The modal excess-loss floor is
\[
F_i^\star(\eta)=\frac12\lambda_iV_i^\star(\eta)=\frac{\eta s_i}{4}+O(\eta^2),
\qquad
\frac{dF_i^\star}{d\eta}=\frac{s_i}{4}+O(\eta),
\]
and perturbations decay as
\[
\left(1-\eta\frac{\lambda_i}{s_i}\right)^2
=
\exp\!\left[-2\eta\frac{\lambda_i}{s_i}+O(\eta^2)\right].
\]
Thus the AdamW specialization of Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral_response_main} is the spectral mixture
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mix}
\Delta L(t)
\approx
\sum_i\frac{s_i}{4}
\sum_{k<t}e^{-2(\lambda_i/s_i)(S_t-S_k)}(\eta_k-\eta_{k+1})_+,
\qquad
\sum_i\frac{s_i}{4}
=
\frac{dF_{\mathrm{eq}}}{d\eta}+O(\eta).
\end{equation}
It predicts a positive lag, an amplitude given by the equilibrium-floor sensitivity, and a
step-time relaxation rate
\[
\tau_i^{-1}=2\eta\frac{\lambda_i}{s_i},
\qquad \tau_i\propto\eta^{-1}.
\]
The classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$ timescale is not itself new; our claim is that this
relaxation controls the residual left by adiabatic loss-curve laws after fast LR decay.

\paragraph{The B-identity: MPL already contains the amplitude.}
The chain above needs the floor sensitivity $\chi=dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$, which we measured
from two-stage probes. It is in fact already encoded in MPL. Compare two schedules that agree
up to step $k$, after which one holds $\eta$ and the other drops to $\eta-d\eta$ and holds.
MPL's annealing term changes by $-B\,d\eta\,G(\eta^{-\gamma}(S_t-S_k))$ and $G\to1$ as
$S_t-S_k\to\infty$, while the $A\,S^{-\alpha}$ backbone difference vanishes at matched $S$.
Hence the \emph{equilibrated} loss change per unit LR drop is exactly $B$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:b_identity}
\left.\frac{\partial L^{\mathrm{MPL}}_{\mathrm{eq}}}{\partial\eta}\right|_{S}=B ,
\end{equation}
i.e.\ MPL structurally encodes a \emph{linear} equilibrium floor with slope $B$. This
identity is falsifiable---$B$ is fit to whole loss curves, the floor slope is measured from
settled two-stage finals---and it holds: $B=363.8/437.9/523.4$ versus measured
$dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta=381.7/499.3/561.5$ at $25/100/400$M ($5$--$15\%$, same
$\sim\!N^{0.13}$ trend; equal at fixed $S$ to $\partial_\eta F_{\mathrm{eq}}$ since the
backbone drops out). Consequence: the correction's amplitude can be predicted from MPL's
\emph{own} parameters, $\kappa=c''\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}B$, with no additional measurements
(\S\ref{sec:exp}).

\paragraph{Second-order closure: the amplitude is $\eta$-dependent.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} froze $\chi(S,\eta)$ to a constant. The derivation itself says more: the
forcing in Eq.~\eqref{eq:Kt_main} is $\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)$, evaluated at the
LR \emph{where the decrement is deposited}. The settled floors are measurably
\emph{superlinear}, $F_{\mathrm{eq}}\propto\eta^{p}$ with $p=1.06/1.49/1.25$ at
$25/100/400$M (three-point fits; qualitative $p>1$), so $\chi(\eta)\propto\eta^{p-1}$ falls
by up to $\sim\!3\times$ over a decade of decay at $100/400$M (negligibly at $25$M). The
second-order one-pole closure weights each decrement by the post-decrement LR:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:law2}
L(t)=L_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t)
+\kappa\sum_{k<t}\Big(\tfrac{\eta_k}{\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}}\Big)^{\!\delta}
e^{-\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}(S_t-S_k)}\,\tfrac{(\eta_{k-1}-\eta_k)_+}{\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}},
\qquad \delta=(p-1)_+ ,
\end{equation}
with $\delta=1/4$ as the default, selected by Pareto dominance on the audited transfer
matrix (\S\ref{sec:exp}), never fit on target curves; the measured per-scale
$(p{-}1)_+$ behaves equivalently, and we do not claim universality of either choice beyond
the audited curves. Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} is the $\delta{=}0$ special case (with
$\kappa=c\,\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\chi$ under the normalized-drop convention of
\S\ref{sec:repro2}).

\paragraph{Where the superlinearity cannot and can come from.}
It cannot come from the same slow noise-dominated modes that set
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$: the exact mode response gives
$\chi_i(\eta)=s_i/(2-\eta\lambda_i/s_i)^2=s_i/4+(\lambda_i/4)\eta+O(\eta^2)$, and with the
measured $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}\sim2\times10^{-3}$ this varies by
$\sim\!0.2\%$ across the decay---three orders of magnitude short. A mechanism consistent
with all observations is a two-population spectrum: a \emph{bulk} of slow modes contributing
the linear floor $\propto\sum_i s_i\eta/4$, plus an \emph{edge} tail of preconditioned rates,
$g(a)\propto a^{-\zeta}$, truncated at the adaptive stability boundary
$a_{\max}\approx2\theta/\eta$ \emph{at the current LR}~\cite{cohen2022}. Substituting
$u=\eta a$ in the edge integral gives
\[
F_{\mathrm{edge}}(\eta)=g_0\,\eta^{\zeta}\!\int_{u_{\min}}^{2\theta}\!\frac{u^{-\zeta}}{2(2-u)}\,du
\;\propto\;\eta^{\zeta}
\]
(the lower cutoff $u_{\min}=\eta a_{\min}$ contributes
$\propto a_{\min}^{1-\zeta}\eta$, which is absorbed into the bulk linear term), hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bulk_edge}
F_{\mathrm{eq}}(\eta)=c_1\eta+c_2\eta^{\zeta},\qquad \zeta\in(1,2),
\end{equation}
whose one-decade power-law fit yields exactly the observed effective $p\in(1,\zeta)$. A
frozen cutoff ($a_{\max}$ pinned at $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$) predicts a near-linear floor
instead, so the two are distinguishable. The non-closed step, stated honestly: in a static
spectrum the edge population relaxes in $O(1)$ steps and would contribute no slow lag; the
conjecture is that the edge \emph{re-pins} to the new boundary on the slow
preconditioner-adaptation timescale, which is the $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ channel. We
claim consistency, not first-principles derivation.

\paragraph{Predictions tested below.}
The theory predicts: (i) a positive residual after LR drops; (ii) a causal dependence on LR
\emph{decrements}, not merely on LR level; (iii) memory in cumulative-LR time; (iv) amplitude
controlled by the fixed-$S$ floor sensitivity $\chi$; (v) in the noise-dominated AdamW regime,
$\tau\propto1/\eta$; (vi) because Eq.~\eqref{eq:mix} is a spectral mixture, a two-pole
kernel can improve diagnostics, while the main predictor deliberately uses the one-pole
closure Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} to avoid overfitting; (vii) by the B-identity, the amplitude is
recoverable from MPL's own parameters; and (viii) if the floor is superlinear, the
second-order closure Eq.~\eqref{eq:law2} should specifically improve \emph{cross-family}
amplitude transfer (probe-calibrated prediction of sharp decays), the regime where freezing
$\chi$ misassigns response across LR ranges.

\cite{luo2025}) is the baseline throughout. We freeze the published MPL and compare it to
MPL$+$Eq.~\eqref{eq:law}; the only addition is the rate-dependent term. Unless noted
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{=}10$.

\paragraph{The rate prediction $\tau\propto1/\eta$ holds (Fig.~\ref{fig:tau}).}
The two-stage \code{wsdcon} curves step the LR down at step $8000$ to a constant
$\eta\!\in\!\{3,9,18\}\!\times\!10^{-5}$, giving a clean relaxation window. Fitting the
residual transient $r(t)\propto e^{-(t-8000)/\tau}$ yields $\tau$ that scales as $\eta^{-p}$
with \emph{pooled} $p=1.00\pm0.18$ (per scale $p=0.51/1.06/0.94$ at $25/100/400$M). This is
the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$ confirmed for the loss-curve-law residual in LLM
pretraining, and---since $p{=}1$ is the noise-dominated value---it self-consistently justifies
the $v_i^*\!\approx\!s_i^2$ step of the derivation. The implied rate constant
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}=1/(\tau\eta)\approx10$ is approximately stable across the public
same-architecture scales; its modes have
$\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}\!\approx\!2\!\times\!10^{-3}\!\ll\!2$ (deep in the
slow regime, far from the edge of stability---unlike $\gamma$).

\paragraph{The residual is the derived term.}
Regressing the well-fit MPL residual on $\Drop_{10}$ (through the origin) gives
$R^2{=}0.40/0.80/0.87$ at $25/100/400$M (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}); a 2-exponential kernel
improves $R^2$ by $0.06\text{--}0.07$, confirming the predicted spectral mixture
(Eq.~\ref{eq:mix}). The form is not arbitrary: in a fair few-shot comparison the derived
S-time exponential kernel cuts held-out MAE by $-15\%$, beating a step-time kernel
($-7\%$, wrong ``time'' variable) and a memoryless floor ($-2\%$), so the cumulative-LR
``time'' and the decaying memory both matter; an S-time kernel driven on the LR
\emph{level} rather than on decrements ties the decrement-driven one ($-15\%$), so this
comparison isolates the time variable and the presence of memory but does \emph{not} by
itself single out decrements as the drive---that choice is fixed by the LR-decrement
response derivation (\S\ref{sec:theory}), not by this test
(\code{repro/generic\_kernel\_compare.py}). (On a \emph{single} decay curve
the finite-$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ kernel is numerically close to the $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{\to}0$
adiabatic limit, so the rate constant is pinned only \emph{across} schedules; \S\ref{sec:repro2}.)

\paragraph{The amplitude is predictable from the noise floor.}
Estimating $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ \emph{independently} from the two-stage final losses
(noise floor vs.\ LR) predicts the fitted $\kappa$ up to a near-constant factor: the ratio
$\kappa_{\mathrm{fit}}/\kappa_{\mathrm{pred}}=0.43/0.51/0.60$ has coefficient of variation
$14\%$. With raw LR drops this gives $c\!\approx\!0.5$; if drops are normalised by
$\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ the same data require $c\,\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\approx0.5$,
i.e.\ $c\approx60$--$86$ (\S\ref{sec:repro2}). In either convention the near-constant ratio
is the transferable quantity (the $<\!1$ factor is the spectral spread of
Eq.~\ref{eq:mix}).

\paragraph{Zero-extra-measurement amplitude: the B-identity chain.}
Replacing the probe slope by MPL's own $B$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:b_identity}) in the same
leave-one-scale-out chain gives
$\kappa_{\mathrm{fit}}/(\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}B)=0.449/0.584/0.646$ (CV $14.7\%$, versus
$13.9\%$ for the probe chain) and held-out sharp-decay MAE improvement of
$\mathbf{-43.1\%}$ ($6/6$ wins)---statistically indistinguishable from the probe chain's
$-44.0\%$, with \emph{zero} additional training runs and zero fitting at the target. The
honest scope: $B$ requires no measurements beyond MPL's published parameters, but MPL's
official fit split contains one two-stage schedule (\code{wsdcon\_9}), so we do not claim
the amplitude is identified from smooth schedules alone. Refitting MPL on smooth-only
splits quantifies this dependence: $B$ itself drifts by up to $30\%$
(the known $\{B,C,\beta,\gamma\}$ refit trade-off), yet the chain retains most of the
gain---$-40.0\%$ (\code{cosine\_24000}$+$\code{constant\_24000}), $-36.8\%$ (two cosines),
$-35.6\%$ (three smooth curves), all $6/6$ wins
(\code{repro/formula\_lab/b\_stability.py}). The amplitude is therefore empirically
recoverable even from smooth-only splits at modestly reduced gain; only the headline
$-43\%$ inherits the official split. Both CVs are three-point statistics.

\paragraph{Low-calibration cross-scale transfer (Table~\ref{tab:xscale}).}

leave-one-scale-out average of the \emph{other} scales, and $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ from the

$\sim\!2\times$ better than MPL transfer.

\paragraph{Calibration audit within the same law.}
We also searched calibration choices while keeping Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} fixed
(\code{repro/current\_law\_calibration\_search.py}). The final target remained the same:
cosine-fit MPL evaluated on \code{wsd}/\code{wsdld}. Fitting only the amplitude $\kappa$ on
the single non-target probe \code{wsdcon\_9} improves the final WSD MAE by $14.5\%$ (6/6
wins) at $\lambda=10$. Fitting $\kappa$ on all three \code{wsdcon} probes improves by
$17.9\%$ (6/6 wins), with the best $\lambda$ in the range $7$--$10$. The stronger
cross-scale chain in Table~\ref{tab:xscale} remains best: scanning
$\lambda\in\{2,3,5,7,10,14,20,30,50\}$ gives $40.5$--$45.0\%$ improvement, with

chosen on the target curves; it is an independently measured, near-optimal value, while the
main gain comes from the theoretically motivated amplitude transfer
$\kappa=c\,\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\chi$.

\paragraph{Final amplitude rule: nuisance-projected empirical Bayes.}
The direct amplitude-transfer story above is useful diagnostically, but it is not the final
calibration rule used in our audit. The final estimator treats the MPL residual
$r=L_{\mathrm{obs}}-L_{\mathrm{MPL}}$ as
\[
r=\kappa_\star\phi+g_\star+\varepsilon,\qquad g_\star\in\mathcal G,\qquad \kappa_\star\ge0,
\]
where $\phi$ is the response feature generated by Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} and $\mathcal G$ is a
low-frequency MPL-residual nuisance subspace. Let $M_{\mathcal G}$ be the orthogonal
residualizer, $\phi_\perp=M_{\mathcal G}\phi$, $r_\perp=M_{\mathcal G}r$, and
$R=\|\phi_\perp\|_2^2/\|\phi\|_2^2$. The cap-free paper-facing amplitude is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kappa_final}
\widehat\kappa
=
\sqrt R
\left(
\frac{\langle \phi_\perp,r_\perp\rangle}
     {\|\phi_\perp\|_2^2+\tau_{\mathrm{EB}}^2}
\right)_+,
\qquad \tau_{\mathrm{EB}}=\sigma/k_0 .
\end{equation}
This is a Frisch--Waugh--Lovell partial-regression coefficient with an empirical-Bayes
ridge denominator and an identifiable-amplitude conversion. The prior/noise ratio
$\tau_{\mathrm{EB}}$ (distinct from the relaxation time $\tau$) is
estimated from calibration curves only; a stricter train-only variant preserves the legacy
smooth-basis result (worst off-diagonal $-2.7\%$, mean $-12.1\%$, cosine$\to$WSD $-5.6\%$).
Across the current cross-schedule transfer matrix, the strongest cap-free implementation of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:kappa_final} gives worst off-diagonal MAE change $-2.7\%$, mean off-diagonal
$-12.1\%$, cosine$\to$WSD $-4.3\%$, and \code{wsdcon\_9}$\to$WSD $-16.0\%$. The optional
hard cap $\kappa\le0.03$ has the same worst case on this basis, so the cap is not the
stabilizing mechanism. Removing the $\sqrt R$ conversion creates a positive off-diagonal
failure ($+1.8\%$), confirming that the raw projected coefficient over-transfers amplitude
from nuisance-confounded feature energy.

\paragraph{Choosing the nuisance subspace.}
The theoretical object is the low-frequency nuisance subspace $\mathcal G$, not a polynomial
loss model. As a schedule-agnostic check, we also define
$\mathcal G_K=\operatorname{span}\{q_0,\ldots,q_K\}$ from discrete-cosine low-frequency
modes and choose the bandwidth by a two-stage rule:
\[
K^\star=\arg\min_{K\ge K_{\min}}\left|
\frac{\|M_{\mathcal G_K}\phi\|_2^2}{\|\phi\|_2^2}-\rho\right|.
\]
The minimum bandwidth enforces drift control; the retention target chooses among sufficiently
rich nuisance spaces. Without $K_{\min}$, the same retention rule selects under-covered
spaces and fails ($+304\%$ worst case). With $K_{\min}=3$ and $\rho=0.35$, the automatic
spectral rule remains non-failing (worst $-1.7\%$, mean $-11.2\%$, cosine$\to$WSD
$-10.1\%$). A balanced fixed choice $K=4$ also remains non-failing (worst $-1.8\%$,
cosine$\to$WSD $-3.6\%$). These spectral variants are slightly more conservative than the
strongest smooth-basis implementation, but they show that the estimator is not tied to a
polynomial-shaped nuisance basis.

\paragraph{Split audit: what the law does \emph{not} claim.}
To avoid mistaking a useful correction for a universal residual patch, we exhaustively varied
the calibration/test split over the five non-cosine public curves
(\code{repro/current\_law\_split\_research.py}; Fig.~\ref{fig:decay_matrix}). When $\lambda$ and $\kappa$ are chosen only
by training residual fit, the median held-out improvement is still positive ($-14.1\%$,
182/225 wins), but there are large failures: calibrating on the two sharp decays and testing
the long \code{wsdcon} tails can worsen MAE by $70$--$96\%$. Conversely, restricting
calibration to the \code{wsdcon} probes and evaluating the final sharp-decay target improves
42/42 scale/probe cases (mean $-14.8\%$). This pins down the valid use case: the law is a
leading-order correction for sharp-cooldown prediction calibrated from probes or cross-scale
amplitude transfer, not a black-box residual smoother for every schedule family.

\paragraph{Matched calibration matters.}
We then asked whether simply using more calibration curves improves the same law
(\code{repro/current\_law\_more\_data\_calibration.py}). In a leave-one-sharp protocol, using
the other full sharp-decay curve to calibrate $\kappa$ predicts the held-out sharp decay very
well: $-49.0\%$ MAE at fixed $\lambda=10$ and $-49.9\%$ when $\lambda$ is selected from the
calibration set (6/6 wins). But adding the three \code{wsdcon} probes to that same sharp
calibration \emph{reduces} the gain to $-19.5\%$ at $\lambda=10$; probes alone give
$-17.6\%$. Thus the issue is not sample count. Calibration curves must match the target
cooldown family, or the fitted amplitude is diluted by long-tail probe dynamics. This result
also separates two regimes: if one full WSD-family curve is already available, the current law
transfers strongly across sharp shapes; if no full WSD curve is available, the safer
low-calibration claim remains the probe/cross-scale protocol in Table~\ref{tab:xscale}.

\paragraph{Second-order amplitude closure: evaluation (Table~\ref{tab:closure}).}
We swept the closure Eq.~\eqref{eq:law2} through every protocol above with
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{=}10$ pinned (Table~\ref{tab:closure}). Three findings. (i) With
the default $\delta{=}1/4$ (selected by this Pareto comparison, not fit on any target),
the audited $6{\times}6$ transfer matrix
(\code{final\_no\_cap} estimator, identical machinery) improves on \emph{every} aggregate:
worst off-diagonal $-2.8\%$ vs $-2.7\%$, mean $-13.5\%$ vs $-12.1\%$,
cosine$\to$WSD $-8.4\%$ vs $-4.3\%$; the per-scale measured $\delta=(p{-}1)_+$ behaves
equivalently (worst $-2.0\%$). (ii) Cheap-calibration deployment strengthens substantially:
$\kappa$ fit on the three \code{wsdcon} probes alone improves the held-out sharp decays by
$-23.0\%$ ($\delta{=}1/4$) to $-28.6\%$ ($\delta{=}1/2$) versus $-17.1\%$ for the
first-order law, and the probe-dilution failure of \S\ref{sec:exp} ($-49\%$ collapsing to
$-19.0\%$ when probes are added to a sharp calibration set; $-19.5\%$ under the original
both-sharp-evaluation variant quoted earlier) recovers to $-25.8/-31.6\%$. (iii) The cost
is bounded and in-family: leave-one-sharp $-46.3\%$ vs $-49.0\%$, the
Table~\ref{tab:xscale} chain $-42.5\%$ vs
$-44.0\%$. An oracle (matched-amplitude) audit shows part of (ii) is amplitude
\emph{recalibration}---the $\le\!1$ weight deflates probe features, inflating the fitted
$\kappa$ toward its sharp-curve value---while at matched amplitude the unweighted shape
still fits sharp in-family residuals marginally better. We therefore present
Eq.~\eqref{eq:law2} as the recommended deployment closure for probe-calibrated and
cross-family use, and Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} as the leading-order law it reduces to at
$\delta{=}0$; $\delta$ is never fit on target curves.

\paragraph{Matched-probe calibration.}
An adversarial adjudication pass over the finished program surfaced one further
calibration rule, which we adopt: when some probe's stage-2 LR equals the target
schedule's \emph{terminal} LR at the specification level (here \code{wsd}/\code{wsdld} end
at $3\times10^{-5}$, exactly \code{wsdcon\_3}'s stage-2), fit $\kappa$ on that probe
alone; otherwise pool. The rule uses only the target's schedule (known a priori), and where
no match exists---and, per the scope gate below, on the entire $10.7$M bed---it reduces to
pooling, leaving those results unchanged. It is physically the statement that the amplitude should be read at the
LR the target relaxes \emph{toward}. Gains on the audited protocols: probes-only
$-23.0\!\to\!-27.0\%$ and dilution $-25.8\!\to\!-30.4\%$ at $\delta{=}1/4$
($-28.8/-32.1\%$ with the measured per-scale $\delta$); the $\delta{=}1/2$ frontier
reaches $-36.8/-39.0\%$, surpassing even the pooled $(\delta,\lambda)$ grid oracle
($-34.5\%$), all $6/6$. The same schedule-level rule extends to the audited
$6{\times}6$ matrix as a target-conditioned override of the shrunk estimator on matched
cells (it never touches cosine rows): at $\delta{=}0$ it is a strict Pareto improvement
(worst $-3.0$ vs $-2.7$, mean $-12.4$ vs $-12.1$), and at $\delta{=}1/4$ it lifts the mean
to $-14.0$ with worst/median unchanged
(\code{repro/formula\_lab/matrix\_matched\_cells.py}). The rule's domain is
floor-curvature-dominated regimes and we gate it on the probe-measured floor exponent
$p>1$: training a deliberately matched probe on the $10.7$M bed
($p_{\mathrm{real}}{=}0.65$ under the audit-corrected design window; $0.73$ as originally
extracted---the gate pools either way)
shows the rule \emph{firing there would hurt} ($-3.8\%$ vs pooled $-71.2\%$ on
\code{sharp600} at $\delta{=}3/4$---the deepest-drop probe has the most
concentration-suppressed amplitude), so below $p{=}1$ the rule pools; the gate uses only
quantities the law already measures. Honesty note: both the match gate and its $p>1$ scope
were identified \emph{after} observing real-model bed behaviour (post-hoc origin
documented, not leakage---no target losses are used). Two sibling proposals from the same
adjudication failed their pre-set gates and are closed: a backlog-saturation amplitude
factor (wrong grading of per-probe ratios at $3/3$ scales; twodrop $A_2/A_1$ outside the
measured band) and a merged step-$+$\,$S$-time clock (its affine form describes the
$10.7$M floor-relaxation transients decisively---AIC $-23.9$ vs $-0.1$---and degenerates
to pure $S$-time at the public scales, but it leaves the probe-vs-sharp rate gap untouched
and its ladder constants \emph{fail retrodiction} of the bed deployment ($-0.6\%$ vs the
deployed $-18.1\%$ on \code{sharp600}), so it is recorded as descriptive only; the
rate-grid step is retained).

\paragraph{Closed structural dead ends.}
Two natural upgrades fail and we close them. \emph{Kernel shape:} replacing the
one-pole kernel by two-exponential, Lomax ($\equiv$ Gamma mixtures of exponentials, the
same family as MPL's $G$), or stretched-exponential kernels collapses back to the single
exponential under joint fitting, both with and without the amplitude weight
($\lambda_1{\to}\lambda_2$, shape${\to}\infty$; probe-calibrated transfer never beats the
matched one-pole in any tested protocol). \emph{Rate unification:} the fitted rate stays
family-dependent ($\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}\!\approx\!15$--$19$ on probes vs
weakly-identified $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}\!\approx\!0.5$--$5$ on
sharp decays) under every weight; $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{=}10$ remains the independently
measured compromise, and the amplitude weight does not (and should not) repair the
single-curve rate degeneracy.

\paragraph{Honest scope: when it does \emph{not} help.}
If instead one refits MPL on a few \emph{full} WSD curves, MPL itself absorbs the residual
(held-out MAE $0.0037\!\to\!0.0032$) and the explicit term adds nothing. So the residual is,
for a data-\emph{rich} MPL, an \emph{information} gap, not a formula gap: the value of the
correction is precisely the data-\emph{poor} regime---cosine plus cheap probes, but no full
WSD curves---which is the actual ``predict before training'' use case.

\paragraph{Relation to $\gamma$: complementary, not a substitute.}
MPL's factor $\eta_k^{-\gamma}$ modulates its annealing kernel by the LR at each decrement:
it tunes the rate-dependence of the (adiabatic) annealing benefit. Our term is the separate
(non-adiabatic) transient, and improves a \emph{full}-$\gamma$ MPL (Table~\ref{tab:xscale})---%
the two are complementary. The freeze-then-correct architecture is itself
load-bearing, not a convenience: refitting all nine parameters \emph{jointly} on the
official split reaches $-53\%$ on the held-out sharp decays but only by warping the
backbone ($\gamma\,0.64\!\to\!1.47$, $B\,364\!\to\!1630$ at $25$M; at $400$M the fit sets
$\kappa\!\to\!0$, absorbing the lag into $\gamma/B$ entirely) and collapsing the smooth
family (\code{cosine\_72000} MAE $+85$ to $+197\%$)---the lag term and $\gamma/B$ are
partially interchangeable in-fit, so only a backbone frozen on its balanced fit keeps both
families (\code{repro/formula\_lab/annexation.py}). Characterising the precise
$\gamma$--lag relationship beyond this is left to future work (\S\ref{sec:disc}).

\section{Independent reproduction}\label{sec:repro2}
An independent reproduction on different hardware (\code{represent/}; the authors' public
curves are no longer hosted, so it \emph{retrains} models) corroborates the mechanism along
two routes the public curves do not cover. All results below were subjected to an adversarial
audit (\code{results/AUDIT\_PARTC.md}): the auditor was tasked to \emph{falsify} each claim,
and only surviving readings are reported.

\paragraph{A real, independently-trained transformer (no fitting).}
A $\sim$10M-parameter Llama-2-style byte-level transformer was trained on WikiText with AdamW
($\beta_1{=}0.9$, $\beta_2{=}0.95$, $\mathrm{wd}{=}0.1$) under three schedules reaching the
\emph{same} final LR ($2.5{\times}10^{-3}{\to}2.5{\times}10^{-4}$) at different speeds
(Table~\ref{tab:real}). The fast (\code{wsd\_sharp}) sweep ends \emph{higher} than the gradual
ones \emph{despite spending $37\%$ more cumulative LR}---yet any law monotone in $S$ predicts
the opposite (more LR $\Rightarrow$ lower loss). With no fitting, this is the rate-dependent
non-adiabatic lag, on a model and data we never used.

A well-fit MPL (trained on \code{constant} plus two \code{wsdcon} curves, held-out MAE
$\approx0.005$) leaves residuals that sort by sweep speed: cosine $-0.011$ $<$ wsd-gradual
$-0.004$ $<$ \textbf{wsd-sharp $+0.006$} (the only positive; Fig.~\ref{fig:real}). Across
schedules, a finite-$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ $\Drop$ kernel improves over the adiabatic
($\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{\to}0$) baseline by $\Delta R^2\!\approx\!+0.18$ on held-out data.
\emph{Note:} on a \emph{single} decay curve the finite-$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ kernel is
numerically degenerate with the adiabatic limit (corr$\approx0.99$); the rate dependence is
identifiable only \emph{across} schedules. The honest claim is therefore the no-fitting
Table~\ref{tab:real} signature plus the $+0.18$ joint advantage, not a single-curve $R^2$.

\paragraph{From-scratch AdamW confirms the mechanism quantitatively.}
Simulating AdamW on a noisy quadratic (Monte-Carlo ground truth; closed-form theory used only
as an independent baseline), the reproduction recovers:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
\item $\tau\propto1/\eta$ with $p=0.971$ (log-log $R^2{=}0.9996$), robust to leave-one-out and
  seed changes;
\item $\tau$ independent of $\beta_2\in[0.9,0.9999]$ (CV $1.1\%$), because the lag lives on
  noise-dominated modes where $v^*\approx s^2$ is constant;
\item the \textbf{amplitude identity} $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta=3.075$ versus the closed form
  $\tfrac14\sum_i s_i=3.0$ (within $3\%$);
\item a two-exponential kernel that recovers the injected preconditioned rates $2\lambda/s$
  ($\{1,16\}$ in the simulation, recovered as $\{0.87,16.1\}$).
\end{itemize}
The effect is falsifiable, not circular: a signal-dominated spectrum breaks $p{=}1$.

\paragraph{Out-of-family schedules on the real transformer.}
To test the upgraded closure where no public curve resembles it, we trained additional
$10.7$M-parameter runs (same recipe, seed-matched): \code{sharp600} (a $600$-step linear
decay to $0.1\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$, sharper than any suite curve), \code{invsqrt}
(a smooth $\eta\propto1/\sqrt{t}$ family seen nowhere else), \code{twodrop} (two
separated instantaneous drops), \code{cyclic} (drop, re-warm to peak, drop), plus a
seed-matched \code{onedrop} counterfactual. Calibrating $\kappa$ and
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ only on the suite's two-stage probes (the selected
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{\approx}1$ matches the independently measured flat
$\tau\!\approx\!850$ steps at this scale) and predicting held-out curves: on the
\emph{fast-decay} targets the second-order closure wins decisively and monotonically in
$\delta$---\code{sharp600} improves $-18.1\%$ ($\delta{=}0$) $\to$ $-61.3\%$
($\delta{=}1/2$) $\to$ $-71.2\%$ ($\delta{=}3/4$), and the suite's own \code{wsd}/\code{wsdld}
improve $-7.6/-6.2\%$ $\to$ $-26.5/-22.2\%$ $\to$ $-36.1/-32.7\%$. The same pattern holds
on a second, separately-trained showcase suite at $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}{=}2.5\times10^{-3}$
(\code{wsd\_sharp}: $-13.8\%\to-52.4\%$ from $\delta{=}0$ to $\delta{=}3/4$). Neither bed
was ever used to select $\delta$. Two boundaries surfaced: on the \emph{smooth}
\code{invsqrt} target the raw probe-calibrated correction over-corrects ($+6\%$ at
$\delta{=}0$, growing with $\delta$)---the adiabatic-regime failure our public-curve audit
machinery exists to gate, whose absolute abstention thresholds do not transfer across
suites without recalibration; and on \code{cyclic} the MPL backbone itself fails after the
re-warm (residual $+0.07$, an order of magnitude above the lag), so rate dependence is not
testable through MPL residuals there. The \code{twodrop} suite measures the deposit ratio
$A_2/A_1$ of the two drops via seed-paired differences
(\code{twodrop}$-$\code{onedrop}, bitwise-identical state at the second drop; the kernel
decomposition through the MPL residual is discarded---its two kernels are collinear at
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}\!\approx\!1$). A single seed is noise-dominated; averaging
\emph{three} seeds, the best-fitting specification family gives
$A_2/A_1=0.86$--$0.92$ (poorly-fitting $\tau$-fixed variants reach $1.7$; we report the
spread). The cross-check: the floor-gap deposit evaluated at the \emph{independently}
ladder-measured floor exponent predicts $(0.5^{p}-0.15^{p})/(1-0.5^{p})=0.89$ at the
originally-extracted $p=0.73$ (inside the measured band) and $\approx0.96$ at the
audit-corrected $p=0.65$ (marginally above it, agreement to ${\sim}5\%$); the strong
weighted closures ($0.30$--$0.38$) remain excluded at this scale either way. Two independent
measurements thus agree through the floor-gap form, confirming that at $10$M the deposit
follows the (sublinear) floor and the $\delta\!>\!0$ transfer gains ride on the
concentration channel.

\paragraph{Equal-$S$ floor ladder: no emergence at a matched recipe.}
Constant-LR floor measurements on the real model were previously confounded: runs with
different stage-2 LRs accumulate different $S$, and the backbone moves opposite to the
noise floor (the earlier non-monotone finals). We remove the confound by construction:
seed-matched runs share an identical trunk to step $3000$, then drop to
$\eta_2\in\{0.5,1,2,3,4,6,8,15\}\times10^{-4}$ with stage-2 lengths chosen so \emph{every
run ends at the same cumulative LR} $S^\star$---backbone terms cancel identically. The
floors are strictly monotone in $\eta_2$ (validating the design) and the post-drop
relaxation is clean per rung. With eight rungs, two seeds, and floors extracted over the
pre-registered design window (the last $25\%$ of stage~2 \emph{in steps}; an earlier
row-fraction extraction inflated the exponent and its CI and is corrected here), the
fitted floor power law at $10.7$M is $F_{\mathrm{eq}}\propto\eta^{0.65}$ ($90\%$ CI
$[0.61,0.68]$): the floor is \emph{sublinear}. We then repeated the identical protocol at
$25$M with the same depth/width recipe and batch size: $F_{\mathrm{eq}}\propto\eta^{0.64}$
($[0.61,0.67]$), and the flat-$\tau$ anomaly persists at both scales
($p_\tau\approx0.1$ and $\approx0$). The exponent value is itself horizon-indexed: a
pre-registered extension to trunks of $12$k and $24$k steps raises it to
$0.77$ and $0.79$ (still strictly sublinear, CI upper $0.81<1$), so the equal-$S$
floor at finite budget is not an equilibrium quantity---but the \emph{direction}
is horizon-robust, sublinear at every horizon out to $24$k, and $p$ never crosses $1$.
The pre-registered emergence test does \emph{not}
fire ($\Delta p=-0.006$ against a $0.067$ line): \textbf{the superlinear exponents on the
public beds ($1.06/1.49/1.25$ at $25/100/400$M) are not reproduced by parameter count at a
matched recipe}. A pre-registered $B{=}192$ ladder excludes batch size as well: it shifts
the floor \emph{level} down substantially (consistent with a noise-floor amplitude
$\propto\eta/B$) while leaving the exponent unchanged ($p=0.61$ $[0.58,0.65]$) --- the
floor's $\eta$-exponent is invariant to the gradient-noise scale on this bed. Whatever
switches the floor superlinear between these beds (data, recipe, schedule
family, or floor protocol---horizon is excluded, our beds staying sublinear out to $24$k)
is neither model size nor batch size alone --- and we have a
primary suspect: re-running the \emph{public} floor protocol (settled fixed-length
\code{wsdcon} probes, three-point fit) on our own beds manufactures strongly superlinear
readings ($p$ pinned at the fit bound ${\approx}3.5$ in the public three-point window)
from the same models whose equal-$S$ ladders measure $0.65$, because fixed-stage-2 probes
re-introduce the cumulative-$S$ confound (their floors are U-shaped in $\eta_2$) that the
equal-$S$ design removes. The public $1.06/1.49/1.25$ exponents therefore carry a
demonstrated mechanism-level protocol caveat, and we leave the bed-level source formally
unlocalized rather than overclaim. Two
consequences stand: the $\delta\!>\!0$ transfer gains on this model cannot be attributed
to floor curvature at either scale and must come from the concentration-dependent visible
amplitude (\S\ref{sec:disc}); and any universality claim for $\delta$ across beds remains
excluded, which is why the closure carries a measured per-scale route. The corrected
exponent also revises the deposit cross-check above: at $p=0.65$ the floor-gap form
predicts $A_2/A_1\approx0.96$, marginally \emph{above} the measured $0.86$--$0.92$ band
--- the two independent measurements agree to ${\sim}5\%$ rather than exactly, and we
report it so.

\paragraph{What does not transfer (consistent with our scope).}
At $\sim$10M the $\tau\propto1/\eta$ relation itself is \emph{not} resolved: the measured
post-step relaxation is roughly flat over a $16\times$ LR range ($\tau\approx475$--$912$
steps; slope $p\approx0.18$), audited as a real measurement and not a window artefact
(synthetic recovery tests and profile-likelihood both reject truncation). This matches the
paper's own statement that the signal grows strongly with scale; at notebook scale we confirm
the \emph{effect} but not its precise scaling law.

A second reproducible issue is a persistent $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ normalisation inconsistency:
if Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} is implemented with raw LR drops, the fitted $c$ is $O(1)$
($\approx0.5$); if normalized drops $(\eta_k-\eta_{k+1})_+/\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ are used, the
prefactor becomes $c\,\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\chi$ and the same data require $c\approx60$--$86$.
The paper's $c\approx0.5$ is therefore tied to a specific (unstated) normalisation convention.

Finally, \code{results/EXTENSIONS\_REPORT.md} tests several theory-driven extensions. The
cleanest success is a \textbf{spectral-mixture} diagnostic: on a bimodal spectrum a
two-exponential kernel improves $R^2$ by $0.065$ and recovers the two theoretical rates
$2\lambda/s$ (relative errors $0.13$ and $0.006$). A cross-shape transfer of frozen
parameters, however, \emph{fails} its pre-registered threshold (mean $R^2{=}0.52$); and the
open problem (a)---computing $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ and $c$ from first principles without
an $O(1)$ fitted constant---remains open.

\subsection{A second adversarial round: final-loss pricing, the batch-size clock, and
direct spectroscopy}\label{sec:round2}
After the first convergence the constraint set changed (a dedicated GPU), which under our
stopping rule re-opens the hunt. Every experiment below was pre-registered (committed
before launch), executed, and then attacked by an independent adversarial audit; we report
the post-audit statements. One audit lesson is methodological and we state it first:
\emph{predictions and measurements must share the evaluation grid}. Our committed derby
predictions were endpoint values while the metric averaged a $200$-step tail window of
still-cooling curves; on late cooldowns the window term alone is ${\sim}15\times10^{-3}$.
All derby numbers below are like-for-like (both sides averaged over the identical
$21$-step eval grid).

\textbf{Does the lag price final loss?} Equal-terminal-LR cooldown derbies (linear
cooldown started at $d_s\in\{1300,3000,5000,5700\}$ of $6000$, three to five seeds,
seed-paired) at both $10.7$M and $25$M. Like-for-like, the mid-late arm fires at both
scales \emph{against the core backbone-prediction ensemble}:
$g(5000)=+7.7\times10^{-3}\pm0.7$ ($10.7$M, $5$ seeds) and
$+8.6\times10^{-3}\pm0.6$ ($25$M) against adiabatic-backbone predictions of
$+3.3$/$+3.7\times10^{-3}$ --- but the fire is ensemble-dependent (null under a widened
seven-split ensemble), the latest arm is \emph{null-consistent} once windows match, and
the fire margins are smaller than the backbone's own demonstrated schedule-shape
systematic at the early-cooldown control ($-6.3\times10^{-3}$).
Our honest statement: late-cooldown final-loss gaps are \emph{consistent} with the
lag-corrected law and the $\delta>0$ closures beat the adiabatic/MPL baselines in relative
comparison ($\chi^2$ $65{\to}17$ at $10.7$M on five seeds; post-hoc there, pre-registered
only at $25$M), but an \emph{absolute} ``lag prices final loss'' claim is not established
at these margins. The $\kappa$ transfer test at $25$M
(zero-measurement B-identity $\kappa_l=\kappa_m B_l/B_m$ vs.\ naive carry-over, both
pre-registered) is closure-dependent --- B-identity wins at $\delta\in\{0,0.5\}$, naive at
$\delta=0.75$ --- so the B-identity chain remains supported for curve-shape transfer
(Table~\ref{tab:xscale}) but is \emph{not separated} for final-loss gap pricing. A
split-free anchor settles why: measuring $\partial L_{\mathrm{eq}}/\partial\eta$ directly
from the equal-$S$ ladder floors gives $B_l/B_m=1.10$, exposing the committed ensemble
mean ($1.80$) as inflated by one unstable backbone fit; at the anchored ratio the
B-identity and naive transfers are indistinguishable on these gaps at every closure.

\textbf{Is the slow-mode clock the optimizer-noise clock?} A batch-size ladder
($B_2\in\{12,24,48,96,192\}\times\eta_2\in\{1,4\}\times10^{-4}$, seed-paired drop/control
arms from a shared bitwise-replayed trunk) tested $\tau\propto B^{-1/2}$ (noise clock)
against $B$-blindness. Window-matched fits (the fit window must not co-vary with $B$ ---
an audit-caught confound) land in the pre-registered \emph{B-blind} bucket under every
common window ($b_B\in[-0.02,+0.12]$, all CIs excluding $-0.5$): the $10.7$M slow mode is
\emph{not} optimizer-noise relaxation, and the deployed step/$S$-clock kernel is the right
default. One robust novel observation rides along: at $\eta_2=10^{-4}$ the benefit of
dropping the LR \emph{inverts} at large batch (paired gap $+0.03$ at $B_2{=}192$ vs.\
$-0.11$ at $B_2{=}12$ at a common horizon; the \emph{sign} replicates across two seeds at
both $B_2\in\{96,192\}$, magnitudes seed-dependent at $B_2{=}96$). The inversion is
quantitatively priced as a horizon-indexed \emph{level} crossing --- the drop arm's fitted
asymptote crosses above the still-descending control at $B^\ast\!\approx\!80$--$86$
(measured interpolated flip $B^\ast{=}86$); a free-exponent law
$\mathrm{gap}(B)=a-b\,B^{-0.20}$ fits all five batches within $0.7\times10^{-3}$, while
$\eta/B$ and $B^{-1/2}$ forms are rejected --- so the benefit of cooling shrinks slowly
with batch, not like the noise variance.

\textbf{Is the slow mode spectral edge re-pinning?} Direct Hessian spectroscopy across
the drop (top-$16$ Lanczos with full reorthogonalization on the AdamW-preconditioned
operator $P^{-1/2}HP^{-1/2}$, fp32 double-backward, fixed probe batches; pre-registered
sanity gate, re-pin ratio, and verdicts). The pre-registered sanity precondition
\emph{fails} at $10.7$M: equilibrated preconditioned sharpness sits at $0.2$--$0.3\times$
the deterministic momentum-corrected edge $38/\eta$ (median over constant-$\eta$ control
probes $0.23$; the observable is volatile, range $13\times$, and the raw-Hessian secondary
channel did not converge --- both disclosed). The scoped conclusion: the
\emph{deterministic} adaptive-edge-of-stability frame does not apply on this bed, so loss
relaxation here is not deterministic-edge re-pinning. The failure \emph{replicates} at
$25$M (ratio $0.38$; the post-drop spectral top even trends \emph{away} from the new edge,
$\rho=-0.80$, while the loss relaxes), so it is not a smallest-scale artifact. We then
tested the natural fallback---re-pinning at a \emph{stochastic} effective threshold---with
a pre-registered constant-$\eta$ equilibration ladder in batch size: if curvature were
noise-regulated, equilibrated sharpness should rise monotonically with $B$. It does not
(non-monotone, $b48>b192$, span only $1.75\times$), and a complementary mode-tracking
analysis finds \emph{no} preconditioned sub-edge mode relaxing on the loss timescale on
any drop arm. The spectral account thus closes at the deterministic edge, the stochastic
edge, and the mode-tracking observable alike; the slow mode remains, on this bed,
phenomenological.

\textbf{Does the rate $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ depend on decrement concentration?} The
rate wanted by instantaneous-drop probes ($\sim$15) and by gradual sharp decays ($\sim$5)
differ, but always across \emph{different} schedule families. A pre-registered
concentration ladder---cool $1.5{\to}0.1\times10^{-3}$ over a width $W\in\{1,10,40,160,640\}$
from a shared trunk, then hold and fit the $S$-time relaxation---isolates the effect within
one bed. Over a wide pre-registered width ladder ($W\in\{1,\dots,5120\}$, $11$ rungs,
concentration $\rho$ spanning $14{\to}5\!\times\!10^{-4}$) the fitted rate is strongly
monotone (Spearman $-0.97$, $\lambda=14.3{\to}1.2$, a $12\times$ span that bridges the
instant-probe and gradual-sharp regimes) and cleanly \emph{log-linear},
$\lambda\approx15.1-3.7\log_{10}W$ ($R^2{=}0.96$; held-out $R^2{=}0.88$ predicting six new
arms from the original five). So the rate gap is a real, large, predictable function of how
concentrated the decrement is. Two pre-registered follow-ups then decide \emph{against}
changing the kernel: (i) a scale-free Mittag-Leffler/fractional memory would make
$\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}(W)$ a \emph{power} law $W^{-(1-\beta)}$, but the power-law fit is far
worse than log-linear ($\Delta\mathrm{AIC}{=}22$), so the trend is not a fractional-memory
fingerprint; (ii) promoting the rate to a width-dependent $\lambda(W)$ improves held-out
relaxation-curve MAE by only $5.9\%$, and heavy-tailed kernels (Lomax, two-exponential,
stretched) collapse to the single pole on the cross-family transfer matrix. A constant
effective $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ is therefore vindicated as the deployment choice; the
non-unification is now a \emph{measured} mild log-linear concentration effect rather than a
mystery, and a closed-form $\lambda(\rho)$ that also earns its held-out keep is left to
future work. The visible \emph{amplitude} channel mirrors the rate channel: on the same
ladder the post-drop excess amplitude also rises monotonically with concentration
(Spearman $+0.90$) but only mildly ($2.2\times$, no clean saturating-gate form,
$R^2{=}0.74$). So neither the rate nor the visible amplitude earns a closed-form
concentration law on this bed; the strong mid-$\eta$ amplitude deficit (probe-to-sharp
effective-$\kappa$ ratio $0.2$--$0.6$) is a public-scale, superlinear-floor phenomenon that
our sublinear $10.7$/$25$M beds cannot validate---a characterization left to a larger
machine, not a single accelerator.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:disc}
The gain is regime-specific (data-poor sharp decays). If full WSD curves are available, a
refit MPL absorbs the residual and the explicit term adds nothing; its value is precisely the
``predict before training'' use case.

$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ and $c$ are \emph{measured}, not computed from first principles
(this needs the noise spectrum $\{s_i\}$, which we do not have). The controlled simulation
shows that $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ tracks the slowest preconditioned mode
$\lambda_i/s_i$ rather than a spectral average, but a robust predictor still requires an
unexplained $O(1)$ constant (open problem (a)). We refine that open problem: $c$ is
measurably \emph{concentration-dependent}---instantaneous drops show a smaller visible
slow-lag amplitude per unit drop (per-curve probe-to-sharp effective-$\kappa$ ratios of
$0.2$--$0.45$, i.e.\ implied $c\!\approx\!0.10$--$0.26$ on the two-stage probes vs
$0.43$--$0.60$ on gradual sharp decays), early-window fits exclude tail contamination as
the cause, and neither the second-order weight nor any tested kernel shape absorbs it. A
nonlinear-relaxation closure ($\dot r=-\lambda_0(1+r/r_\ast)r$ in $S$-time, the linear law
at $r_\ast\!\to\!\infty$) supplies a mechanism: in joint family fits one parameter set then
reconciles probes and sharp decays (sharp $R^2$ $0.08\!\to\!0.35$ at $100$M,
$0.52\!\to\!0.65$ at $400$M), but $r_\ast$ is unidentifiable within any single schedule
family and its cross-scale transfer ($-23\%$) is dominated by the simpler
Eq.~\eqref{eq:law2} ($-29\%$), so we report it as mechanism, not as the predictor
(\code{repro/formula\_lab/nonlinear\_ode.py}). The
$\eta$-dependent part of the amplitude is captured by Eq.~\eqref{eq:law2}; the
concentration-dependent part of $c$ remains open. A related caveat: at $25$M, where the lag
signal is weakest ($R^2{=}0.40$, $\tau$-slope $0.51$, $p{-}1{=}0.06$) and the kernel is
near-degenerate with the adiabatic limit on single curves, part of the reported gain may be
adiabatic floor repair rather than the non-adiabatic mechanism; the $6/6$ win counts should
be read with this caveat.

The floor exponents $p$ are three-point log-log fits whose backbone subtraction omits the
$B$-annealing term; we use them as qualitative evidence for $p>1$ \emph{at the public
scales}, not as a quantitative
confirmation of any specific $\delta$ (at $25$M, $p{-}1=0.06$ is consistent with no
superlinearity, and the confound-free equal-$S$ ladders give \emph{sublinear} exponents
$0.65/0.64/0.61$ at $10.7$M, $25$M matched-recipe, and $10.7$M-$B{=}192$
respectively---the exponent is \emph{bed}-dependent, not parameter-count- or
batch-size-dependent, and the source of the public-bed superlinearity is not localized;
\S\ref{sec:repro2}, \S\ref{sec:round2}). $\delta{=}1/2$, although strongest for probe-only calibration, was first
identified in an in-sample scan over the public curves and is therefore reported but not
claimed as a pre-registered result; the paper-facing default $\delta{=}1/4$ was selected by
Pareto dominance on the audited matrix, not by target fitting. The bulk$+$edge mechanism
(\S\ref{sec:theory}, Eq.~\eqref{eq:bulk_edge}) is consistent with the data but its slow
edge-re-adaptation step is a conjecture.

The quantitative $\tau\propto1/\eta$ relation, clean in the controlled simulation
($p{=}0.971$), is \emph{not} resolved on the $\sim$10M transformer (\S\ref{sec:repro2}),
consistent with the paper's statement that the signal grows with scale. On that model the
finite-$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ kernel is numerically degenerate with the adiabatic baseline
on a single curve, so the rate dependence is identifiable only \emph{across} schedules.

There is a reproducible $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ normalisation ambiguity
(\S\ref{sec:repro2}): retaining the $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ factor in Eq.~\eqref{eq:law}
gives $c\approx60$--$86$, not the $c\approx0.5$ reported in the main experiments. The two
conventions are equivalent, but the universality claim for $c$ depends on which normalisation is
adopted.

The general linear-response derivation assumes local stability and differentiability of an
LR-dependent excess-loss state; the stronger locally quadratic, diagonal-noise assumptions are
used only for the AdamW specialization. Cross-shape transfer of frozen parameters fails its
pre-registered threshold, and the relationship between our correction and MPL's empirical
$\gamma$ remains qualitative. All real-data experiments are on one public dataset at three
scales; broader scales and architectures are the natural next step.

\section{Conclusion}
Existing loss-curve laws are adiabatic approximations. We identified and validated the leading
non-adiabatic correction: under weak stability assumptions it is a causal linear response to LR
decrements, and its one-pole closure gives a rate-dependent term whose relaxation time follows
the classical $\tau\propto1/\eta$ ($p=1.00\pm0.18$, confirmed for the loss-curve residual).
Its empirically stable shape enables low-calibration cross-scale transfer ($-44\%$ on held-out
sharp decays in the public same-architecture setting). We then upgraded the law's amplitude
structure: the B-identity shows MPL's own annealing coefficient \emph{is} the floor
sensitivity the correction needs (amplitude transfer from MPL's published parameters with
no additional measurements, $-43\%$), and the public-bed superlinear floor reading (shown
in \S\ref{sec:round2} to be settled-probe-protocol-dependent---confound-free equal-$S$
ladders read sublinear---rather than a bed-independent law) motivates a
second-order closure
$\chi(\eta)\propto\eta^{\delta}$ that improves the audited transfer matrix on every
aggregate and strengthens cheap-probe calibration by $35$--$70\%$ relative, with the
amplitude-vs-shape decomposition of that gain reported honestly. Two structural dead ends (multi-pole kernel shapes;
cross-family rate unification) are closed. An independent reproduction---a separately-trained
$\sim$10M transformer and a from-scratch AdamW simulation---confirms the effect (the fast
sweep ends higher \emph{despite} $37\%$ more cumulative LR) and the amplitude identity
(within $3\%$), and out-of-family schedules (double-drop superposition, cyclic re-warm,
inverse-sqrt, sharper decay) plus an equal-cumulative-LR floor ladder test the upgraded
closure beyond the public schedule families, while also establishing
honest boundaries: the quantitative $\tau\propto1/\eta$ law is not resolved at $\sim$10M, the
kernel is degenerate on single curves, and an $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ normalisation ambiguity
remains. The loss curve depends not only on how much LR has been spent, but on how fast it is
being spent.

\section{Linear-response proof details}\label{app:linear_response}
This appendix gives the detailed derivation behind \S\ref{sec:theory}. The goal is not to
prove a theorem for full neural-network training, but to make precise the weak local
conditions under which the LR-decrement convolution follows. The conclusion is a
first-order, finite-window expansion around a stable moving equilibrium.

\paragraph{Formal local assumptions.}
We work on a finite-dimensional active subspace controlling the excess loss during the decay
window. Let $z_t$ obey $z_{t+1}=\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}(z_t)$ and let
$z^\star(S,\eta)$ be a fixed point. We assume:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
\item[(A1)] $\Phi_{S,\eta}$, $z^\star(S,\eta)$, and $\ell$ have bounded first and second
  derivatives in a tube of radius $R$ around the moving equilibrium.
\item[(A2)] The linearized products are stable: for $k<t$,
  $\|T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\|\le q_{k,t}$ with $\sum_{k<t}q_{k,t}\le Q$ on the finite window.
\item[(A3)] The LR decrement size $\Delta=\max_k|\eta_{k+1}-\eta_k|$ and the tracking error
  remain small enough that the Taylor expansions stay inside the tube.
\item[(A4)] The slow $S$-drift forcing is not the cooldown-specific object of interest. We
  denote it by $\epsilon^S_k$ and carry it in the error rather than absorbing it into the LR
  decrement term.
\item[(A5)] For the sign corollary only, there is a cone $\mathcal C$ such that
  $\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)\in\mathcal C$, $T_u\mathcal C\subseteq\mathcal C$, and
  $D\ell_t[v]\ge0$ for all $v\in\mathcal C$.
\end{itemize}
A1--A4 are enough for the convolutional response. A5 is only a clean sufficient condition for
positivity.

\paragraph{Tracking-error recursion.}
Define
\[
\delta_t=z_t-z^\star(S_t,\eta_t),
\qquad
T_t=D_z\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}\big(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)\big).
\]
By Taylor expansion and the fixed-point identity,
\begin{align}
 z_{t+1}
 &=\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}\big(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)+\delta_t\big) \\
 &=z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)+T_t\delta_t+R_t,
\end{align}
with $\|R_t\|\le C\|\delta_t\|^2$. Subtracting
$z^\star(S_{t+1},\eta_{t+1})$ gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app_delta0}
\delta_{t+1}=T_t\delta_t+
\big[z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)-z^\star(S_{t+1},\eta_{t+1})\big]+R_t.
\end{equation}
Expand the moving fixed point:
\begin{align}
z^\star(S_{t+1},\eta_{t+1})
&=z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)
  +\partial_Sz^\star(S_t,\eta_t)(S_{t+1}-S_t)
  +\partial_\eta z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)(\eta_{t+1}-\eta_t)
  +R_t^\star,
\end{align}
where $\|R_t^\star\|\le C(|S_{t+1}-S_t|^2+|\eta_{t+1}-\eta_t|^2)$. Since the first-order
$S$-drift is not the cooldown-specific LR-decrement forcing, define
\[
\epsilon_t^S=-\partial_Sz^\star(S_t,\eta_t)(S_{t+1}-S_t),
\]
after removing the component already modeled by the adiabatic backbone. Keeping the LR-drop
forcing gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app_delta_rec}
\delta_{t+1}=T_t\delta_t+
\partial_\eta z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)\Delta\eta_t^+ + e_t,
\end{equation}
where
\[
\|e_t\|\le C\big(\|\delta_t\|^2+|\Delta\eta_t|^2+\|\epsilon_t^S\|+\eta_t^2\big).
\]
The $\eta_t^2$ term is harmless on the finite window and accounts for the mismatch between
one-step discrete dynamics and the first-order continuous-time approximation used below.

\paragraph{Duhamel expansion and error.}
Iterating Eq.~\eqref{eq:app_delta_rec} yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app_duhamel}
\delta_t
=
T_{t-1}\cdots T_0\delta_0
+
\sum_{k<t}T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)\Delta\eta_k^+
+
\sum_{k<t}T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}e_k.
\end{equation}
Linearizing the observable gives
\[
r_t=F_t-F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S_t,\eta_t)
=D\ell_t[\delta_t]+O(\|\delta_t\|^2)+\epsilon_{\mathrm{MPL}},
\]
where $\epsilon_{\mathrm{MPL}}$ is present when MPL is used as a surrogate for the ideal
adiabatic component. Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:app_duhamel} gives Eq.~\eqref{eq:main_response}
with
\[
K_{k,t}=D\ell_t\!\left[T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)\right]
\]
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app_error_bound}
|\mathcal E_t|
\le
C\sum_{k<t}q_{k,t}
\big(\|\delta_k\|^2+|\Delta\eta_k|^2+\|\epsilon_k^S\|+\eta_k^2\big)
+C\|\delta_t\|^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{MPL}} .
\end{equation}
If the decay begins after a long stable phase, the initial transient
$D\ell_t[T_{t-1}\cdots T_0\delta_0]$ is exponentially or summably small under A2; otherwise it
is a separate transient and should not be attributed to the cooldown drop response.

\paragraph{Cumulative-LR time.}
Now add the first-order optimizer approximation
\[
T_u=I-\eta_uA+E_u,
\qquad
\|E_u\|\le C(\eta_u^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}),
\]
with bounded stable $A$. Since the factors $I-\eta_uA$ commute when $A$ is fixed,
\begin{align}
\prod_{u=k+1}^{t-1}(I-\eta_uA)
&=\exp\left(\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\log(I-\eta_uA)\right) \\
&=\exp\left(-A\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u+O\left(\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u^2\right)\right) \\
&=\exp\left(-A(S_t-S_k)\right)
  +O\left(\eta_t+\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u^2\right),
\end{align}
where the last step uses boundedness of $A$ on the active subspace ($\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}
\eta_u=S_{t-1}-S_k$; replacing $S_{t-1}$ by $S_t$ costs the $O(\eta_t)$ term shown).
Including $E_u$ adds
$O(\sum_u\eta_u^2+\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}})$, giving Eq.~\eqref{eq:product_exp_main}.
If the scalar response $D\ell_t[e^{-xA}\partial_\eta z^\star]$ is represented by a positive
Laplace mixture, then the kernel has the form in Eq.~\eqref{eq:spectral_response_main}. The
single-pole predictor in Eq.~\eqref{eq:law} is the rank-one, lowest-capacity closure of this
general stable kernel.

\paragraph{Positivity.}
Under A5, $\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)\in\mathcal C$ and
$T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\mathcal C\subseteq\mathcal C$, so
$K_{k,t}\ge0$. If $\Delta\eta_k^+\ge0$, the leading response is positive. This formalizes the
observed sign: after the LR is dropped, the equilibrium floor falls before the actual
excess-loss state has relaxed, so the true loss lies above the adiabatic predictor.
