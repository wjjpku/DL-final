\begin{abstract}
State-of-the-art laws for predicting the pretraining loss curve under a learning-rate
(LR) schedule---the Multi-Power Law (MPL) and the Functional Scaling Law (FSL)---are
\emph{adiabatic}: they predict the quasi-static loss the schedule would reach at
equilibrium. MPL does use the LR decrements (a convolution of $\Delta\eta_k$ with a
saturating kernel), but its only handle on the \emph{rate} of decay is the single empirical
exponent $\gamma$; calibrated on slow schedules, this cannot capture the transient by which
the loss \emph{lags} its equilibrium when the LR is annealed fast. The result is a
systematic \emph{positive} residual on \emph{fast} (warmup-stable-decay, WSD) decays, which
we identify as a non-adiabatic relaxation lag. The
classical SGD relaxation time governing this lag, $\tau\sim 1/(\eta\lambda)$, is well known;
our contribution is to (i) show it governs the residual of adiabatic loss-curve laws, (ii)
derive the resulting correction in closed form from AdamW's preconditioned second-moment
dynamics---a convolution of the per-step LR decrements with a cumulative-LR (S-time)
exponential kernel, $\Drop$, whose amplitude equals the exactly-identified equilibrium-floor
sensitivity $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$---and (iii) turn it into a usable predictor. We confirm
in LLM pretraining that the residual relaxes with the predicted $\tau\propto1/\eta$
(measured pooled exponent $p=1.00\pm0.18$), which also self-consistently establishes the
noise-dominated regime the derivation assumes. Because the kernel's rate constant is a
\emph{scale-invariant preconditioned curvature}, the correction can be \emph{predicted, not
fitted}, and transferred across model size: with zero fitting on the target curve it improves
held-out sharp-decay loss prediction by $28\text{--}56\%$ ($44\%$ on average). The correction
also explains MPL's one unexplained empirical exponent, $\gamma$, as its implicit
low-capacity surrogate. We are candid about scope: the gain is in the regime where WSD curves
are unavailable; given them, a refit MPL absorbs the residual.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The learning-rate schedule shapes the \emph{entire} loss trajectory of LLM
pretraining~\cite{kaplan2020,hoffmann2022,hu2024minicpm}. \emph{Schedule-aware loss-curve
prediction} asks: given $\{\eta_t\}$, predict $L(t)$, so that schedules can be compared and
optimized \emph{before} spending compute. The defining test is cross-schedule
generalization---fit on cosine schedules, predict the unseen WSD family. The state of the art
is the \textbf{Multi-Power Law} (MPL)~\cite{luo2025}, with a matching kernel-regression
theory, the \textbf{Functional Scaling Law} (FSL)~\cite{li2025fsl}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mpl}
L(t)=L_0+A\,S(t)^{-\alpha}+B\!\sum_{k\le t}\!\Delta\eta_k\,
G\!\big(\eta_k^{-\gamma}(S(t)-S(k))\big),\quad G(x)=1-(1+Cx)^{-\beta},
\end{equation}
where $S(t)=\sum_{i\le t}\eta_i$ and $\Delta\eta_k=\eta_{k-1}-\eta_k$.

\paragraph{The gap: existing laws are adiabatic.}
These laws \emph{do} use the LR decrements: MPL convolves $\Delta\eta_k$ with a saturating
kernel $G$, and its factor $\eta_k^{-\gamma}$ is a rate-of-decay knob. But this single
empirical exponent is their \emph{only} handle on how \emph{fast} the LR changes.
Structurally they predict the \emph{quasi-static} loss---the value the schedule would reach
if the loss stayed at equilibrium; the river-valley analysis~\cite{wen2024} makes this
explicit, modelling the noise penalty as set \emph{instantaneously} by the current LR. We
call such laws \emph{adiabatic}. Calibrated on slow schedules, $\gamma$ does not extrapolate
to fast WSD decays, where the loss genuinely lags equilibrium---leaving a structured
residual (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}) that no single rate exponent captures across schedules. Our
correction is the derived transient that $\gamma$ only crudely surrogates.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
\item We show that adiabatic loss-curve laws leave a systematic \emph{positive} residual on
fast decays, and identify it as a non-adiabatic relaxation lag (\S\ref{sec:resid}).
\item From AdamW's preconditioned second-moment dynamics we \emph{derive} the leading
non-adiabatic correction in closed form, the rate-dependent term $\Drop$, with amplitude
equal to the exactly-identified $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ (\S\ref{sec:theory}). The relaxation
time it uses is the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$; our point is that it governs the
\emph{loss-curve-law residual}, not that the timescale is new.
\item We confirm in LLM pretraining that the residual relaxes with $\tau\propto1/\eta$
($p=1.00\pm0.18$), self-consistently establishing its noise-dominated regime
(\S\ref{sec:exp}).
\item The kernel's rate constant is a scale-invariant preconditioned curvature, enabling a
\emph{parameter-free, cross-scale} prediction that improves held-out sharp-decay loss by
$44\%$ with no target-curve fitting (\S\ref{sec:exp}).
\item The correction explains MPL's empirical $\gamma$ as its implicit surrogate, and we give
a fair-baseline diagnostic separating a genuine formula gain from an information gap
(\S\ref{sec:exp},\ref{sec:rel}).
\end{itemize}
All experiments use the public MPL loss curves (Llama-2-style $25/100/400$M, $9$ schedules
each)~\cite{luo2025}; a faithfully reproduced MPL (test MAE $0.0054/0.0050/0.0085$, matching
\cite{luo2025}) is the baseline throughout.

\section{The non-adiabatic residual}\label{sec:resid}
We take a \emph{well-fit} MPL (the published parameters~\cite{luo2025}) and examine its
prediction residual $r(t)=L_{\mathrm{true}}(t)-L_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t)$ on held-out schedules.
(Isolating the non-adiabatic term requires a well-fit MPL: a baseline that already captures
the \emph{adiabatic} part cleanly, so the remainder is the lag and not a backbone mis-fit; a
cosine-only fit conflates the two and gives a far noisier residual.) The residual has a sharp
signature (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}): it is $\approx 0$ throughout the long stable phase and on
gradual cosine decay, but jumps \emph{positive} exactly when the LR decays steeply, and is
largest---and \emph{undamped at the curve's end}---for the sharpest decays (\code{wsd},
\code{wsdld}). The end-of-curve lag is $+(6\text{--}15)\times10^{-3}$ on WSD/WSDLD versus
$\lesssim\!4\times10^{-3}$ on cosine. Crucially, a gradual cosine and a sharp WSD decay reach
the \emph{same} final LR, yet only the fast one lags: same destination, faster sweep, bigger
lag---the textbook signature of a rate-dependent (non-adiabatic) effect, and exactly the
dependence an adiabatic law cannot represent.

\section{Theory: the leading non-adiabatic correction}\label{sec:theory}
\paragraph{MPL is the adiabatic limit.}
At a fixed LR the loss relaxes toward a quasi-static value $L_{\mathrm{eq}}(\eta)$ (backbone
progress plus the equilibrium variance of the sharp ``mountain'' directions of the
river-valley landscape~\cite{wen2024}). On schedules that vary the LR slowly the loss tracks
$L_{\mathrm{eq}}$, so \textbf{a well-fit MPL approximates the adiabatic $L_{\mathrm{eq}}$} (its
kernel, in particular $\gamma$, is calibrated to reproduce the quasi-static component). The
residual on \emph{fast} held-out decays is the non-adiabatic remainder.

\paragraph{Derivation (AdamW).}
In the Hessian eigenbasis, mode $i$ has curvature $\lambda_i$ and gradient-noise variance
$s_i^2$. With $\beta_2\!\to\!1$ the Adam preconditioner sits at
$v_i^*=\mathbb E[g_i^2]=\lambda_i^2V_i+s_i^2$, and in the noise-dominated mountain directions
$v_i^*\!\approx\!s_i^2$, so Adam reduces to SGD with a \emph{preconditioned} step
$\eta_i^{\mathrm{eff}}=\eta/s_i$~\cite{malladi2022,cohen2022}. The second moment
$V_i=\mathbb E[\delta_i^2]$ then obeys the \emph{exact} recursion
$V_i(t{+}1)=(1-\eta_i^{\mathrm{eff}}\lambda_i)^2V_i(t)+(\eta_i^{\mathrm{eff}})^2s_i^2$, with
fixed point $V_i^*(\eta)$ and the classical relaxation rate $2\eta_i^{\mathrm{eff}}\lambda_i$,
i.e. $\tau_i\sim 1/(\eta\,\lambda_i/s_i)$~\cite{zhang2019nqm}. Writing the deviation
$\Delta_i=V_i-V_i^*(\eta_t)$ and using
$(1-\eta^{\mathrm{eff}}\lambda)^2\!\approx\!e^{-2\eta^{\mathrm{eff}}\lambda}$ (valid because
the lag lives on \emph{slow} modes, $\eta\lambda_i/s_i\!\ll\!1$, verified post hoc), the
linear recursion solves to a cumulative-LR (S-time) exponential:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:exact}
\Delta_i(t)=\frac{dV_i^*}{d\eta}\sum_{t'}e^{-2(\lambda_i/s_i)(S_t-S_{t'})}\,\mathrm{drop}_{t'},
\qquad \mathrm{drop}_{t'}=(\eta_{t'-1}-\eta_{t'})_+ .
\end{equation}
The loss lag $\Delta L=\tfrac12\sum_i\lambda_i\Delta_i$ is therefore a $w_i$-weighted
\emph{mixture} of S-time exponentials with rates $2\lambda_i/s_i$, and the total amplitude is
an \emph{exact identity}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mix}
\Delta L(t)=\sum_i w_i\big[e^{-2(\lambda_i/s_i)(S-S')}\!\circledast\mathrm{drop}\big](t),
\qquad \sum_i w_i=\frac{dL_{\mathrm{eq}}}{d\eta}.
\end{equation}
Collapsing the spectrum to one effective rate gives the operational law (one shape constant
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$, one calibration $c\!\le\!1$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:law}
\boxed{\;L(t)=L_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t)+c\cdot\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\frac{dL_{\mathrm{eq}}}{d\eta}\cdot
\underbrace{\sum_{t'}e^{-\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}(S_t-S_{t'})}\,\mathrm{drop}_{t'}}_{\Drop_{\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}}(t)}\;}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{What the derivation predicts (all tested in \S\ref{sec:exp}).}
(i) \emph{Sign}: $dV_i^*/d\eta>0$, so $\Delta L>0$ (loss \emph{above} MPL).
(ii) \emph{Rate}: the per-step rate is $2(\eta/s_i)\lambda_i\propto\eta$, hence
$\tau\propto1/\eta$; a self-consistent treatment of the preconditioner gives
$\tau\propto\eta^{-p}$ with $p{=}1$ for noise-dominated modes and $p{=}2/3$ if
signal-dominated. (iii) \emph{Amplitude}: $\sum w_i=dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$, measurable from
the noise floor. (iv) \emph{Shape}: $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}=2(\lambda/s)_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is a
\emph{preconditioned} curvature---the quantity Adam stabilizes~\cite{cohen2022}---so it is
roughly scale/architecture invariant. (v) \emph{Spectral mixture}: Eq.~\eqref{eq:mix} is a
sum of exponentials, so a two-exponential kernel should beat one.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:exp}
We freeze the published MPL and compare it to MPL$+$Eq.~\eqref{eq:law}; the only addition is
the rate-dependent term. Unless noted $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{=}10$.

\paragraph{(i) The rate prediction $\tau\propto1/\eta$ holds (Fig.~\ref{fig:tau}).}
The two-stage \code{wsdcon} curves step the LR down at step $8000$ to a constant
$\eta\!\in\!\{3,9,18\}\!\times\!10^{-5}$, giving a clean relaxation window. Fitting the
residual transient $r(t)\propto e^{-(t-8000)/\tau}$ yields $\tau$ that scales as $\eta^{-p}$
with \emph{pooled} $p=1.00\pm0.18$ (per scale $-1.06/-0.94/-0.51$ at $100/400/25$M). This is
the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$ confirmed for the loss-curve-law residual in LLM
pretraining, and---since $p{=}1$ is the noise-dominated value---it self-consistently justifies
the $v_i^*\!\approx\!s_i^2$ step of the derivation. The implied rate constant
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}=1/(\tau\eta)\approx10$ is scale-invariant; its modes have
$\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}\!\approx\!2\!\times\!10^{-3}\!\ll\!2$ (deep in the
slow regime, far from the edge of stability---unlike $\gamma$).

\caption{Loss-relaxation time $\tau$ measured from \code{wsdcon} decay transients vs.\

\paragraph{(i$'$) The same scaling holds in a from-scratch AdamW simulation.}
To check that $\tau\propto1/\eta$ is the mechanism and not a data artefact, we simulate
AdamW on a noisy quadratic, equilibrate at a high LR, step it down, and fit the
loss-relaxation time (\code{repro/beta2\_ablation.py}). Sweeping the post-step LR reproduces
$\tau\propto1/\eta$ with log-log slope $p=1.01$. The same simulation also \emph{rules out} a
$\beta_2$ relaxation channel: sweeping $\beta_2\in[0.9,0.9999]$ leaves $\tau$ flat
(CV $2\%$), because the lag lives on noise-dominated modes where the Adam preconditioner
$v\!\approx\!s^2$ is constant. (The $\beta_2$ sensitivity of cooldown reported by
Dremov~et~al.~\cite{dremov2025} is an \emph{outcome}/bias--variance effect, distinct from the
relaxation time.) The two-exponential improvement in (ii) is therefore the curvature-spectral
spread, not $\beta_2$.

\paragraph{(ii) The residual is the derived term.}
Regressing the well-fit MPL residual on $\Drop_{10}$ (through the origin) gives
$R^2{=}0.40/0.80/0.87$ at $25/100/400$M (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}); a 2-exponential kernel
improves $R^2$ by $0.06\text{--}0.07$, confirming the predicted spectral mixture
(Eq.~\ref{eq:mix}).

\paragraph{(iii) The amplitude is predictable from the noise floor.}
Estimating $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ \emph{independently} from the two-stage final losses
(noise floor vs.\ LR) predicts the fitted $\kappa$ up to a near-constant factor: the ratio
$\kappa_{\mathrm{fit}}/\kappa_{\mathrm{pred}}=0.43/0.51/0.60$ has coefficient of variation
$14\%$, i.e.\ $c\!\approx\!0.5$ is universal (the $<\!1$ is the spectral spread of
Eq.~\ref{eq:mix}).

\paragraph{(iv) Parameter-free, cross-scale prediction (Table~\ref{tab:xscale}).}
Because $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ and $c$ are scale-invariant and $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ is
read off the (cheap) noise floor, the whole correction can be \emph{predicted with zero
fitting on the target curve}: take $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{=}10$, $c$ from a
leave-one-scale-out average of the \emph{other} scales, and $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ from the
target's own two-stage probes (distinct curves from the test). Applied to the held-out
\code{wsd}/\code{wsdld} decays it cuts MAE by $28\text{--}56\%$ ($44\%$ average, wins
$6/6$)---small-model constants plus a cheap probe predict the large-model sharp-decay curve
$\sim\!2\times$ better than MPL transfer.

\paragraph{(v) Honest scope: when it does \emph{not} help.}
If instead one refits MPL on a few \emph{full} WSD curves, MPL itself absorbs the residual
(held-out MAE $0.0037\!\to\!0.0032$) and the explicit term adds nothing. So the residual is,
for a data-\emph{rich} MPL, an \emph{information} gap, not a formula gap: the value of the
correction is precisely the data-\emph{poor} regime---cosine plus cheap probes, but no full
WSD curves---which is the actual ``predict before training'' use case.

\paragraph{(vi) Relation to $\gamma$.}
MPL's empirical factor $\eta_k^{-\gamma}$ gives its kernel a weak dependence on the LR at a
decrement---an \emph{implicit, low-capacity} handle on exactly this non-adiabatic lag. This
explains both why $\gamma$ is essential ($3\text{--}4\times$ worse without it) yet underivable
from the (adiabatic) spectral theory, and why a data-rich MPL can absorb the residual through
$\gamma$ while a few-shot one cannot.

\section{Related work}\label{sec:rel}
\paragraph{Loss-curve laws.}
Tissue~et~al.~\cite{tissue2024}, MPL~\cite{luo2025}, and FSL~\cite{li2025fsl} predict the
trajectory from integrated-LR statistics (cumulative LR / intrinsic time / convolutional
functionals). FSL and its follow-ups also derive optimal schedules~\cite{li2025fsl}. All are
adiabatic in our sense---functions of integrals of $\{\eta_t\}$---and our rate-dependent term
is the leading correction they omit; it also gives MPL's empirical $\gamma$ a mechanism.

\paragraph{Cooldown dynamics and schedule geometry.}
The river-valley picture~\cite{wen2024} explains qualitatively why decay ``reveals
progress'', and explicitly models the noise penalty as \emph{instantaneous} in the current
LR---the adiabatic assumption we refine. Dremov~et~al.~\cite{dremov2025} study the cooldown
empirically and, notably, find that raising AdamW's $\beta_2$ during cooldown helps and that
the second-moment timescale interacts with the decay; this is consistent with the second,
$\beta_2$-dependent relaxation channel our theory predicts (\S\ref{sec:disc}), though they
frame it as a bias--variance trade-off rather than a non-adiabatic lag. Late-decay
analyses~\cite{kaplan2020} and schedule-shape searches~\cite{naganuma2026} study related
questions (why a long high-LR phase helps; what optimal shapes look like) but do not model the
loss's rate dependence.

\paragraph{SGD relaxation physics.}
That a quadratic mode relaxes with $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$ and that the SGD noise floor
scales with $\eta$ are classical, from noisy-quadratic and Langevin analyses of
SGD~\cite{zhang2019nqm}; adaptive methods inherit a preconditioned version~\cite{malladi2022},
operating at an adaptive edge of stability~\cite{cohen2021,cohen2022}. We do not claim these
ingredients as new. Our contribution is the synthesis: identifying that adiabatic loss-curve
laws miss exactly this relaxation lag, deriving the correction with an exactly-identified
amplitude, and building a parameter-free cross-scale predictor from it.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:disc}
The gain is regime-specific (data-poor sharp decays); $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ and $c$ are
\emph{measured}, not computed from first principles (this needs the noise spectrum $\{s_i\}$,
which we do not have); and the derivation assumes a locally quadratic landscape with a static
spectrum and diagonal, uncorrelated noise---the same model-level assumptions MPL itself rests
on. All real-data experiments are on one public dataset at three scales (the simulation of
\S\ref{sec:exp}(i$'$) is a controlled cross-check, not a substitute); broader scales and
architectures are the natural next steps.

\section{Conclusion}
Existing loss-curve laws are adiabatic approximations. We identified, derived from AdamW's
preconditioned dynamics, and validated the leading non-adiabatic correction: a rate-dependent
term whose relaxation time follows the classical $\tau\propto1/\eta$ ($p=1.00\pm0.18$,
confirmed for the loss-curve residual) and whose scale-invariant shape enables parameter-free
cross-scale prediction ($-44\%$ on held-out sharp decays). The loss curve depends not only on
how much LR has been spent, but on how fast it is being spent.
