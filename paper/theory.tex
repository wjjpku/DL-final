\begin{abstract}
State-of-the-art laws for predicting the LLM pretraining loss curve under a learning-rate
(LR) schedule---the Multi-Power Law (MPL) and the Functional Scaling Law (FSL)---are
\emph{adiabatic}: they predict the quasi-static loss a schedule would reach at equilibrium,
depending on the schedule only through its integrated history. We show this leaves a
systematic \emph{positive} residual on \emph{fast} (warmup-stable-decay) decays---a
non-adiabatic relaxation lag, the loss falling behind its equilibrium when the LR is annealed
faster than it can relax. From AdamW's preconditioned second-moment dynamics we derive the
leading correction in closed form: the per-step LR decrements convolved with a cumulative-LR
exponential kernel, whose amplitude is tied by linear response to an equilibrium-floor sensitivity
and whose relaxation time is the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$. On public LLM curves the
residual indeed relaxes with $\tau\propto1/\eta$ ($p=0.84\pm0.17$); since the kernel's rate
constant is approximately scale-invariant, the correction can be \emph{predicted, not fitted},
and transferred across model size, cutting held-out sharp-decay error by $44\%$ with no
target-curve fitting. An independent reproduction---a separately-trained $\sim$10M transformer
and a from-scratch AdamW simulation---confirms the effect (the fast sweep ends higher
\emph{despite} $37\%$ more cumulative LR) and the amplitude identity (within $3\%$). The
correction complements MPL's exponent $\gamma$; its gain is in the data-poor, leading-order
regime.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The learning-rate schedule shapes the \emph{entire} loss trajectory of LLM
pretraining~\cite{kaplan2020,hoffmann2022,hu2024minicpm}. \emph{Schedule-aware loss-curve
prediction} asks: given $\{\eta_t\}$, predict $L(t)$, so that schedules can be compared and
optimized \emph{before} spending compute. The defining test is cross-schedule
generalization---fit on cosine schedules, predict the unseen WSD family. The state of the art
is the \textbf{Multi-Power Law} (MPL)~\cite{luo2025}, with a matching kernel-regression
theory, the \textbf{Functional Scaling Law} (FSL)~\cite{li2025fsl}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mpl}
L(t)=L_0+A\,S(t)^{-\alpha}+B\!\sum_{k\le t}\!\Delta\eta_k\,
G\!\big(\eta_k^{-\gamma}(S(t)-S(k))\big),\quad G(x)=1-(1+Cx)^{-\beta},
\end{equation}
where $S(t)=\sum_{i\le t}\eta_i$ and $\Delta\eta_k=\eta_{k-1}-\eta_k$.

\paragraph{The gap: existing laws are adiabatic.}
These laws \emph{do} use the LR decrements: MPL convolves $\Delta\eta_k$ with a saturating
kernel $G$, and its factor $\eta_k^{-\gamma}$ is a rate-of-decay knob. But this single
empirical exponent is their \emph{only} handle on how \emph{fast} the LR changes.
Structurally they predict the \emph{quasi-static} loss---the value the schedule would reach
if the loss stayed at equilibrium; the river-valley analysis~\cite{wen2024} makes this
explicit, modelling the noise penalty as set \emph{instantaneously} by the current LR. We
call such laws \emph{adiabatic}. Calibrated on slow schedules, $\gamma$ does not extrapolate
to fast WSD decays, where the loss genuinely lags equilibrium---leaving a structured
residual (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}) that no single rate exponent captures across schedules. Our
correction is a derived transient \emph{complementary} to $\gamma$, which tunes the adiabatic
kernel.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
\item We show that adiabatic loss-curve laws leave a systematic \emph{positive} residual on
fast decays, and identify it as a non-adiabatic relaxation lag (\S\ref{sec:resid}).
\item From AdamW's preconditioned second-moment dynamics we \emph{derive} the leading
non-adiabatic correction in closed form, the rate-dependent term $\Drop$, with amplitude
equal, to leading order, to $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ (\S\ref{sec:theory}). The relaxation
time it uses is the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$; our point is that it governs the
\emph{loss-curve-law residual}, not that the timescale is new.
\item We confirm in LLM pretraining that the residual relaxes with $\tau\propto1/\eta$
($p=0.84\pm0.17$), self-consistently establishing its noise-dominated regime
(\S\ref{sec:exp}).
\item The kernel's rate constant is an approximately scale-invariant preconditioned curvature,
enabling a \emph{parameter-free, cross-scale} prediction that improves held-out sharp-decay
loss by $44\%$ with no target-curve fitting (\S\ref{sec:exp}).
\item We give a fair-baseline diagnostic separating a genuine formula gain from an
information gap, clarifying exactly when the correction helps (\S\ref{sec:exp},\ref{sec:rel}).
\end{itemize}
All experiments use the public MPL loss curves (Llama-2-style $25/100/400$M, $9$ schedules
each)~\cite{luo2025}; a faithfully reproduced MPL (test MAE $0.0054/0.0050/0.0085$, matching
\cite{luo2025}) is the baseline throughout.

\section{The non-adiabatic residual}\label{sec:resid}
We take a \emph{well-fit} MPL (the published parameters~\cite{luo2025}) and examine its
prediction residual $r(t)=L_{\mathrm{true}}(t)-L_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t)$ on held-out schedules.
(Isolating the non-adiabatic term requires a well-fit MPL: a baseline that already captures
the \emph{adiabatic} part cleanly, so the remainder is the lag and not a backbone mis-fit; a
cosine-only fit conflates the two and gives a far noisier residual.) The residual has a sharp
signature (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}): it is $\approx 0$ throughout the long stable phase and on
gradual cosine decay, but jumps \emph{positive} exactly when the LR decays steeply, and is
largest---and \emph{undamped at the curve's end}---for the sharpest decays (\code{wsd},
\code{wsdld}). The end-of-curve lag is $+(6\text{--}15)\times10^{-3}$ on WSD/WSDLD versus
$\lesssim\!4\times10^{-3}$ on cosine. Crucially, a gradual cosine and a sharp WSD decay reach
the \emph{same} final LR, yet only the fast one lags: same destination, faster sweep, bigger
lag---the textbook signature of a rate-dependent (non-adiabatic) effect, and exactly the
dependence an adiabatic law cannot represent.

\section{Theory: the leading non-adiabatic correction}\label{sec:theory}

The residual in \S\ref{sec:resid} has two empirical features that a useful theory must
explain: it is triggered by LR decrements, and it is positive after a sharp decay. We derive
these features from a weak moving-equilibrium assumption. The main statement is not that the
optimizer is exactly a noisy quadratic system. Rather, if the LR-dependent excess-loss state
has a stable quasi-static fixed point, then a changing LR necessarily produces a causal
linear response to LR decrements. The exponential cumulative-LR kernel used in our predictor
is the lowest-capacity, one-pole closure of this general response. AdamW's noisy-quadratic
calculation is used only afterward as a mechanistic specialization that explains the observed
amplitude and relaxation-time scalings.

\paragraph{Definitions.}
Let
\[
S_t=\sum_{u\le t}\eta_u
\]
denote cumulative LR. We decompose the loss into a quasi-static schedule-aware component and
a non-adiabatic residual,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loss_decomp_tight}
L_t=L_{\mathrm{ad}}(S_t,\eta_t)+r_t.
\end{equation}
In experiments, a well-fit MPL is used as an empirical surrogate for
$L_{\mathrm{ad}}$, so $L_t-L_{\mathrm{MPL},t}$ estimates $r_t$ up to the remaining MPL
approximation error.

We model the LR-dependent excess-loss state by a hidden variable $z_t$ in a finite-dimensional
active subspace $\mathcal Z$ with norm $\|\cdot\|$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hidden_dyn_tight}
z_{t+1}=\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}(z_t).
\end{equation}
The state $z_t$ may be interpreted as a covariance/noise-floor state, but the derivation below
only uses stability and differentiability. For fixed $(S,\eta)$, let $z^\star(S,\eta)$ be the
quasi-static state and
\[
F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S,\eta)=\ell\big(z^\star(S,\eta)\big)
\]
be the corresponding excess-loss floor. The derivative
\begin{equation}\label{eq:chi_def_tight}
\chi(S,\eta)
:=
\left.\partial_\eta F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S,\eta)\right|_S
=
D\ell_{z^\star(S,\eta)}\big[\partial_\eta z^\star(S,\eta)\big]
\end{equation}
is always taken at fixed cumulative progress $S$. This is the quantity estimated from
independent equilibrium-floor probes.

Define the tracking error around the moving fixed point by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:delta_def_tight}
\delta_t=z_t-z^\star(S_t,\eta_t),
\end{equation}
and define the positive LR decrement by
\[
\Delta\eta_t^+=(\eta_t-\eta_{t+1})_+.
\]

\paragraph{Assumptions.}
The linear-response statement uses the following assumptions over the decay window.

\emph{A1. Local fixed point and smoothness.} For each $(S,\eta)$ in the window,
$z^\star(S,\eta)$ satisfies
\[
z^\star(S,\eta)=\Phi_{S,\eta}\big(z^\star(S,\eta)\big),
\]
and $\Phi$, $z^\star$, and $\ell$ are twice continuously differentiable in a neighborhood of
this trajectory. Their first and second derivatives are uniformly bounded in the window.

\emph{A2. Linear stability.} Let
\[
T_t=D_z\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}\big(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)\big).
\]
The products of the linearized dynamics are uniformly stable and summable: there exist
constants $M<\infty$ and a nonnegative summable envelope $q_{k,t}$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:A2_weak_stability}
\|T_{t-1}T_{t-2}\cdots T_{k+1}\|\le M q_{k,t},
\qquad
\sup_t\sum_{k<t}q_{k,t}<\infty.
\end{equation}
This assumption only says that old perturbations are forgotten. It does not yet impose
cumulative-LR time; that will be derived below from an additional first-order optimizer
structure.

\emph{A3. Decay-specific forcing.} The total moving-target forcing has two components:
one from progress in $S$ and one from changes in $\eta$. We do not model the $S$-drift with
the cooldown correction. Instead, define
\[
\epsilon_t^S
:=
-\partial_S z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)(S_{t+1}-S_t)
+\epsilon_{t,\mathrm{slow}},
\]
where $\epsilon_{t,\mathrm{slow}}$ includes the associated slow-drift approximation and
Taylor-remainder terms. This $S$-drift response is present for both slow and fast schedules
and is therefore part of the background adiabatic/backbone approximation error rather than
the decay-localized residual we seek to model. We therefore analyze the component of the
linear response forced by LR changes. For schedules with LR increments, the omitted increment
component enters with the opposite sign; the predictor below is the cooldown-specific
projection onto LR decrements.

\emph{A4. Small perturbations.} The LR changes and local tracking error are small enough that
second-order Taylor remainders are controlled. We write
\[
\Delta_t=|\eta_t-\eta_{t+1}|,
\qquad
\eta_{\max}=\max_t\eta_t,
\]
and retain terms of order $\Delta_t^2$, $\|\delta_t\|^2$, and the slow drift error as
remainders.

\emph{A5. Positive response cone.} For the sign conclusion, assume there exists a cone
$\mathcal C\subset\mathcal Z$ such that
\[
\partial_\eta z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)\in\mathcal C,
\qquad
T_t\mathcal C\subseteq\mathcal C,
\qquad
D\ell_{z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)}[v]\ge0\quad\text{for all }v\in\mathcal C.
\]
This is not needed for the convolution form; it is only used to conclude that a monotone LR
decay produces a positive residual.

\paragraph{Proposition 1: linear response to LR decrements.}
Under A1--A4, the cooldown-specific excess-loss residual admits the expansion
\begin{equation}\label{eq:prop1_statement_tight}
r_t
=
I_t
+
\sum_{k<t}K_{k,t}\Delta\eta_k^+
+
\mathcal E_t,
\end{equation}
where the initial-transient term is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:initial_transient_tight}
I_t
=
D\ell_{z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)}
\big[T_{t-1}T_{t-2}\cdots T_{0}\delta_0\big],
\end{equation}
and the linear-response kernel is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kernel_general_tight}
K_{k,t}
=
D\ell_{z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)}
\left[
T_{t-1}T_{t-2}\cdots T_{k+1}
\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)
\right].
\end{equation}
Moreover, for constants depending only on the derivative bounds and the stability envelope,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:error_bound_tight}
|\mathcal E_t|
\le
C\sum_{k<t}q_{k,t}
\Big(\Delta_k^2+\|\delta_k\|^2+\|\epsilon_k^{S}\|\Big)
+
\epsilon_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t).
\end{equation}
Here $\epsilon_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t)=L_{\mathrm{ad}}(S_t,\eta_t)-L_{\mathrm{MPL},t}$ is zero for
the ideal adiabatic predictor and is the remaining backbone approximation error when MPL is
used in practice. If the decay starts near the quasi-static state, then $\delta_0$ is small
and the initial transient $I_t$ is negligible after the stability envelope has decayed. In the
perturbative regime, stability implies
\[
\sup_k\|\delta_k\|
=
O\!\left(\sup_k \Delta_k+\sup_k\|\epsilon_k^S\|\right),
\]
so the $\|\delta_k\|^2$ contribution in \eqref{eq:error_bound_tight} is second order in the
forcing size.

\emph{Proof.}
By definition,
\[
\delta_{t+1}=z_{t+1}-z^\star(S_{t+1},\eta_{t+1}).
\]
Using \eqref{eq:hidden_dyn_tight} and Taylor expanding around
$z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)$ gives
\begin{align}
 z_{t+1}
 &=\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}\big(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)+\delta_t\big)\nonumber\\
 &=\Phi_{S_t,\eta_t}\big(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)\big)
   +T_t\delta_t+R_t^{z}\nonumber\\
 &=z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)+T_t\delta_t+R_t^{z},
\end{align}
where $\|R_t^{z}\|\le C\|\delta_t\|^2$ by A1. Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:delta_pre_tight}
\delta_{t+1}
=
T_t\delta_t
+\big[z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)-z^\star(S_{t+1},\eta_{t+1})\big]
+R_t^{z}.
\end{equation}
Now expand the moving fixed point:
\begin{align}
 z^\star(S_{t+1},\eta_{t+1})
 &=z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)
 +\partial_S z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)(S_{t+1}-S_t)
 +\partial_\eta z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)(\eta_{t+1}-\eta_t)
 +R_t^{\star},
\end{align}
with
\[
\|R_t^{\star}\|\le C\big((S_{t+1}-S_t)^2+\Delta_t^2\big).
\]
The $S$-drift contribution and its local Taylor remainder are placed in $\epsilon_t^{S}$.
Projecting the LR-change forcing onto cooldown decrements gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:delta_rec_tight}
\delta_{t+1}
=
T_t\delta_t
+\partial_\eta z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)\Delta\eta_t^+
+\xi_t,
\end{equation}
where
\[
\|\xi_t\|\le C\big(\Delta_t^2+\|\delta_t\|^2+\|\epsilon_t^{S}\|\big).
\]
Iterating \eqref{eq:delta_rec_tight} yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:duhamel_tight}
\delta_t
=
T_{t-1}\cdots T_0\delta_0
+
\sum_{k<t}T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}
\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)\Delta\eta_k^+
+
\sum_{k<t}T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}\xi_k.
\end{equation}
Finally,
\begin{align}
F_t-F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S_t,\eta_t)
&=\ell\big(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)+\delta_t\big)-\ell\big(z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)\big)\nonumber\\
&=D\ell_{z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)}[\delta_t]+O(\|\delta_t\|^2).
\end{align}
Substituting \eqref{eq:duhamel_tight} gives
\eqref{eq:prop1_statement_tight}--\eqref{eq:kernel_general_tight}; applying the stability
envelope \eqref{eq:A2_weak_stability} to the remainder terms gives
\eqref{eq:error_bound_tight}. \hfill$\square$

\paragraph{Corollary 1: sign of the cooldown residual.}
Under A5, if the equilibrium floor increases with LR, namely
\[
\chi(S,\eta)=\left.\partial_\eta F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S,\eta)\right|_S>0,
\]
then $K_{k,t}\ge0$. Hence, for a monotone LR decay and negligible initial transient,
\[
r_t\ge0
\]
to leading order. This is the sign observed in \S\ref{sec:resid}: after a fast LR drop, the
quasi-static floor has moved downward, while the optimizer's excess-loss state relaxes only
gradually, leaving the true loss above the adiabatic predictor.

\paragraph{Proposition 2: cumulative-LR time from first-order contraction.}
Proposition~1 gives a causal kernel but not its time coordinate. Suppose, in addition to
A1--A4, that over the active finite-dimensional subspace
\begin{equation}\label{eq:first_order_contraction_tight}
T_t=I-\eta_t A+R_t^{A},
\qquad
\|R_t^{A}\|\le C\eta_t^2+C\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}},
\end{equation}
where $A$ is a bounded stable operator and $\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}$ bounds the variation of
the local geometry during the decay window. Then, by a standard product-perturbation
bound for the stable product $\prod_u(I-\eta_u A)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:product_exp_tight}
T_{t-1}\cdots T_{k+1}
=
\exp\!\left[-A(S_t-S_k)\right]
+
O\!\left(\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u^2+(S_t-S_k)\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}
+\eta_t\right).
\end{equation}
Consequently the leading kernel depends on $k,t$ through cumulative LR distance, up to the
endpoint convention in the definition of $S_t$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:S_kernel_operator_tight}
K_{k,t}
\approx
D\ell_{z^\star(S_t,\eta_t)}
\left[
\exp\!\left[-A(S_t-S_k)\right]
\partial_\eta z^\star(S_k,\eta_k)
\right].
\end{equation}
If, for the scalar observable $D\ell[\cdot]$, the active response of $A$ admits a positive
Laplace representation,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:laplace_assumption_tight}
D\ell\big[e^{-Ax}\partial_\eta z^\star\big]
=
\int e^{-ax}\,d\rho(a),
\qquad x\ge0,
\end{equation}
then Proposition~1 becomes the spectral response law
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral_response_tight}
r_t
\approx
I_t+
\sum_{k<t}
\underbrace{\int e^{-a(S_t-S_k)}\,d\rho(a)}_{K(S_t-S_k)}
\Delta\eta_k^+
+
\mathcal E_t.
\end{equation}
The instantaneous amplitude of this kernel is fixed by the floor sensitivity:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K0_chi_tight}
K(0)
=
D\ell_{z^\star}\big[\partial_\eta z^\star\big]
=
\left.\partial_\eta F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S,\eta)\right|_S
=\chi(S,\eta).
\end{equation}

\emph{Proof.}
For the unperturbed product, $A$ is fixed and bounded on the active subspace, so
\[
\log(I-\eta_uA)=-\eta_uA+O(\eta_u^2)
\]
uniformly for small $\eta_u\|A\|$. Since the factors $I-\eta_uA$ commute,
\[
\prod_{u=k+1}^{t-1}(I-\eta_uA)
=
\exp\!\left[-A\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u\right]
+
O\!\left(\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u^2\right).
\]
The perturbations $R_u^A$ are handled by the variation-of-constants identity for products:
replacing one factor at a time and using stability gives an additional
$O((S_t-S_k)\epsilon_{\mathrm{drift}}+\sum_u\eta_u^2)$ contribution. Finally,
\[
\sum_{u=k+1}^{t-1}\eta_u=S_t-S_k-\eta_t
\]
under the convention $S_t=\sum_{u\le t}\eta_u$, giving \eqref{eq:product_exp_tight} up to
the endpoint term $O(\eta_t)$. The Laplace-mixture form \eqref{eq:laplace_assumption_tight}
is an additional scalar-response assumption, not a consequence of stability alone. Evaluating
it at $x=0$ gives \eqref{eq:K0_chi_tight} by the chain rule. \hfill$\square$

\paragraph{One-pole closure: the predictor used in experiments.}
The spectral kernel in \eqref{eq:spectral_response_tight} is the primitive theoretical
object. Learning an unrestricted $K$ would add many degrees of freedom and risk fitting
residual curves rather than testing the mechanism. We therefore use the lowest-order stable
closure of a causal relaxation kernel: a single pole in cumulative-LR time. Over the decay
window we also approximate the fixed-$S$ sensitivity by a constant
$\chi\approx\chi(S_{\mathrm{decay}},\eta)$ estimated from independent floor probes. Thus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:one_pole_tight}
K(x)\approx c\,\chi\,e^{-\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}x},
\qquad x\ge0,
\end{equation}
where $c=O(1)$ is a global projection factor; empirically $c<1$ in our fits, consistent with
fitting the persistent tail of a spectral mixture rather than matching $K(0)$ exactly.
Substituting \eqref{eq:one_pole_tight} into \eqref{eq:spectral_response_tight}, neglecting
the initial transient after equilibration, gives the operational correction
\begin{equation}\label{eq:law}
\boxed{\;
L(t)=L_{\mathrm{MPL}}(t)
+
c\,\chi\,
\underbrace{
\sum_{k<t}
e^{-\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}(S_t-S_k)}
(\eta_k-\eta_{k+1})_+
}_{\Drop_{\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}}(t)}
\;}
\end{equation}
with
\[
\chi=
\left.\partial_\eta F_{\mathrm{eq}}(S,\eta)\right|_{S=S_{\mathrm{decay}}}.
\]
If $\Drop$ is instead defined using normalized drops
$(\eta_k-\eta_{k+1})_+/\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$, then the prefactor becomes
$c\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\chi$. This is only a normalization convention; with raw LR drops, the
amplitude has the units and interpretation of a fixed-$S$ equilibrium-floor sensitivity.

\paragraph{AdamW specialization.}
The previous propositions do not require a quadratic loss or diagonal dynamics. We now show
that, in the standard noise-dominated AdamW quadratic reduction, the same kernel has the
amplitude and relaxation scaling used above. This calculation ignores momentum transients,
bias correction, decoupled weight decay, the $\epsilon$ term, and the finite-time dynamics of
$v_t$; it should be read as a local effective-mode calculation, not as an exact model of the
full optimizer.

Consider a Hessian eigenmode with curvature $h_i$ and gradient-noise standard deviation
$s_i$:
\[
g_{i,t}=h_i x_{i,t}+\xi_{i,t},
\qquad
\mathbb E[\xi_{i,t}]=0,
\qquad
\mathbb E[\xi_{i,t}^2]=s_i^2.
\]
In a noise-dominated mountain direction, AdamW's second-moment preconditioner satisfies
$v_i^\star\approx s_i^2$, so the effective scalar update is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adamw_scalar_update_tight}
x_{i,t+1}
=
\left(1-\eta_t\frac{h_i}{s_i}\right)x_{i,t}
-
\eta_t\frac{1}{s_i}\xi_{i,t}.
\end{equation}
Let $V_{i,t}=\mathbb E[x_{i,t}^2]$. At fixed LR $\eta$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Vi_rec_tight}
V_{i,t+1}
=
\left(1-\eta\frac{h_i}{s_i}\right)^2V_{i,t}
+\eta^2.
\end{equation}
Solving for the fixed point,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Vi_star_tight}
V_i^\star(\eta)
=
\frac{\eta^2}{1-\left(1-\eta h_i/s_i\right)^2}
=
\frac{\eta s_i}{2h_i}+O(\eta^2).
\end{equation}
The corresponding excess-loss floor is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Fi_star_tight}
F_i^\star(\eta)
=
\frac12h_iV_i^\star(\eta)
=
\frac{\eta s_i}{4}+O(\eta^2),
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:amp_identity_tight}
\left.\frac{dF_i^\star}{d\eta}\right|_S
=
\frac{s_i}{4}+O(\eta).
\end{equation}
Perturbations around $V_i^\star$ decay by
\[
\left(1-\eta\frac{h_i}{s_i}\right)^2
=
\exp\!\left[-2\eta\frac{h_i}{s_i}+O(\eta^2)\right],
\]
so the S-time relaxation rate of this mode is
\[
a_i=2\frac{h_i}{s_i}.
\]
Thus the mode contributes
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adamw_mode_response_tight}
\Delta L_i(t)
\approx
\frac{s_i}{4}
\sum_{k<t}
e^{-2(h_i/s_i)(S_t-S_k)}
\Delta\eta_k^+.
\end{equation}
Summing over modes gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mix}
\Delta L(t)
\approx
\sum_i\frac{s_i}{4}
\sum_{k<t}
e^{-2(h_i/s_i)(S_t-S_k)}
\Delta\eta_k^+,
\qquad
\sum_i\frac{s_i}{4}
=
\left.\frac{\partial F_{\mathrm{eq}}}{\partial\eta}\right|_S+O(\eta).
\end{equation}
This is the AdamW noisy-quadratic specialization of the spectral-response form
\eqref{eq:spectral_response_tight}. In constant LR step time, the same mode has relaxation
rate
\[
\tau_i^{-1}=2\eta\frac{h_i}{s_i},
\qquad
\tau_i\propto \eta^{-1}.
\]
The classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta h)$ scaling is therefore not new; the point is that this
relaxation time governs the residual left by adiabatic loss-curve laws after a fast LR decay.
The single-rate correction \eqref{eq:law} is the one-pole projection of the spectral mixture
\eqref{eq:mix}, chosen to add the minimum capacity while preserving the sign, trigger,
amplitude, and intrinsic-time structure predicted by linear response.


\section{Experiments}\label{sec:exp}
We freeze the published MPL and compare it to MPL$+$Eq.~\eqref{eq:law}; the only addition is
the rate-dependent term. Unless noted $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{=}10$.

\paragraph{The rate prediction $\tau\propto1/\eta$ holds (Fig.~\ref{fig:tau}).}
The two-stage \code{wsdcon} curves step the LR down at step $8000$ to a constant
$\eta\!\in\!\{3,9,18\}\!\times\!10^{-5}$, giving a clean relaxation window. Fitting the
residual transient $r(t)\propto e^{-(t-8000)/\tau}$ yields $\tau$ that scales as $\eta^{-p}$
with \emph{pooled} $p=0.84\pm0.17$ (per scale $p=0.51/1.06/0.94$ at $25/100/400$M;
\code{repro/deep\_tau\_pooled.py}). This is
the classical $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$ confirmed for the loss-curve-law residual in LLM
pretraining, and---since $p{=}1$ is the noise-dominated value---it self-consistently justifies
the $v_i^*\!\approx\!s_i^2$ step of the derivation. The implied rate constant
$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}=1/(\tau\eta)\approx10$ is scale-invariant; its modes have
$\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}}\!\approx\!2\!\times\!10^{-3}\!\ll\!2$ (deep in the
slow regime, far from the edge of stability---unlike $\gamma$).

(A from-scratch AdamW simulation reproduces the same scaling, $p{=}0.97$; we defer it to the
independent reproduction in \S\ref{sec:repro2}, which covers it in more detail.)

\paragraph{The residual is the derived term.}
Regressing the well-fit MPL residual on $\Drop_{10}$ (through the origin) gives
$R^2{=}0.40/0.80/0.87$ at $25/100/400$M (Fig.~\ref{fig:resid}); a 2-exponential kernel
improves $R^2$ by $0.06\text{--}0.07$, confirming the predicted spectral mixture
(Eq.~\ref{eq:mix}). The form is not arbitrary: in a fair few-shot comparison the derived
S-time exponential kernel cuts held-out MAE by $-15\%$, beating a step-time kernel
($-7\%$, wrong ``time'' variable) and a memoryless floor ($-2\%$), so the cumulative-LR
``time'' and the decaying memory both matter; an S-time kernel driven on the LR
\emph{level} rather than on decrements ties the decrement-driven one ($-15\%$), so this
comparison isolates the time variable and the presence of memory but does not by itself
single out decrements as the drive---that choice is fixed by the LR-decrement response
derivation, not by this test (\code{repro/generic\_kernel\_compare.py}). (On a \emph{single} decay curve
the finite-$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ kernel is numerically close to the $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{\to}0$
adiabatic limit, so the rate constant is pinned only \emph{across} schedules; \S\ref{sec:repro2}.)

\paragraph{The amplitude is predictable from the noise floor.}
Estimating $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ \emph{independently} from the two-stage final losses
(noise floor vs.\ LR) predicts the fitted $\kappa$ up to a near-constant factor: the ratio
$\kappa_{\mathrm{fit}}/\kappa_{\mathrm{pred}}=0.43/0.51/0.60$ has coefficient of variation
$14\%$, i.e.\ $c\!\approx\!0.5$ is universal (the $<\!1$ is the spectral spread of
Eq.~\ref{eq:mix}).

\paragraph{Parameter-free, cross-scale prediction (Table~\ref{tab:xscale}).}
Because $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ and $c$ are approximately scale-invariant across these (same-architecture)
scales and $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ is read off the (cheap) noise floor, the whole correction can
be \emph{predicted with zero fitting on the target curve}: take $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{=}10$, $c$ from a
leave-one-scale-out average of the \emph{other} scales, and $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta$ from the
target's own two-stage probes (distinct curves from the test). Applied to the held-out
\code{wsd}/\code{wsdld} decays it cuts MAE by $28\text{--}56\%$ ($44\%$ average, wins
$6/6$)---small-model constants plus a cheap probe predict the large-model sharp-decay curve
$\sim\!2\times$ better than MPL transfer.

\paragraph{Honest scope: when it does \emph{not} help.}
If instead one refits MPL on a few \emph{full} WSD curves, MPL itself absorbs the residual
(held-out MAE $0.0037\!\to\!0.0032$) and the explicit term adds nothing. So the residual is,
for a data-\emph{rich} MPL, an \emph{information} gap, not a formula gap: the value of the
correction is precisely the data-\emph{poor} regime---cosine plus cheap probes, but no full
WSD curves---which is the actual ``predict before training'' use case.

\paragraph{Relation to $\gamma$: complementary, not a substitute.}
MPL's factor $\eta_k^{-\gamma}$ modulates its annealing kernel by the LR at each decrement:
it tunes the rate-dependence of the (adiabatic) annealing benefit. Our term is the separate
(non-adiabatic) transient, and improves a \emph{full}-$\gamma$ MPL (Table~\ref{tab:xscale}) ---
the two are complementary. Characterising their precise relationship is left to future work
(\S\ref{sec:disc}).

\section{Independent reproduction}\label{sec:repro2}
An independent reproduction on different hardware (\code{represent/}; the authors' public
curves are no longer hosted, so it \emph{retrains} models) corroborates the mechanism along
two routes the public curves do not cover.

\paragraph{A real, independently-trained transformer (no fitting).}
A $\sim$10M-parameter Llama-2-style byte-level transformer was trained on WikiText with AdamW
under three schedules reaching the \emph{same} final LR ($2.5{\times}10^{-3}{\to}2.5{\times}10^{-4}$)
at different speeds (Table~\ref{tab:real}). The fast (\code{wsd\_sharp}) sweep ends
\emph{higher} than the gradual ones \emph{despite spending $37\%$ more cumulative LR}---yet any
law monotone in $S$ predicts the opposite (more LR $\Rightarrow$ lower loss). With no fitting,
this is the rate-dependent non-adiabatic lag, on a model and data we never used; the residual
against a well-fit MPL is positive only for the sharp sweep and is captured by $\Drop$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:real}). Across schedules a finite-$\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ kernel beats the
adiabatic ($\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}{\to}0$) baseline by $\Delta R^2\!\approx\!+0.18$ held out.

\paragraph{From-scratch AdamW confirms the mechanism quantitatively.}
Simulating AdamW on a noisy quadratic (Monte-Carlo ground truth; closed-form theory used only
as an independent baseline), the reproduction recovers $\tau\propto1/\eta$ with $p=0.971$
(log-log $R^2{=}0.9996$, robust to leave-one-out and seeds), $\tau$ independent of $\beta_2$
(CV $1.1\%$), the \textbf{amplitude identity} $dL_{\mathrm{eq}}/d\eta=3.075$ versus the closed
form $\tfrac14\sum_i s_i=3.0$ (within $3\%$), and a two-exponential kernel that recovers the
injected preconditioned rates $2\lambda/s$. The effect is falsifiable, not circular: a
signal-dominated spectrum breaks $p{=}1$.

\paragraph{What does not transfer (consistent with our scope).}
At $\sim$10M the $\tau\propto1/\eta$ relation itself is \emph{not} resolved (the measured
post-step relaxation is roughly flat over a $16\times$ LR range, audited as real and not a
window artefact), and on a \emph{single} decay curve the kernel is numerically degenerate with
the adiabatic baseline, so the rate-dependence is identifiable only \emph{across} schedules.
This matches our stated scope: the effect is clean and present, while the quantitative law is a
leading-order, data-scale-limited approximation whose constants ($\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$, $c$,
and the $\eta_{\mathrm{peak}}$ normalisation) remain measured rather than derived.

\section{Related work}\label{sec:rel}
\paragraph{Loss-curve laws.}
Tissue~et~al.~\cite{tissue2024}, MPL~\cite{luo2025}, and FSL~\cite{li2025fsl} predict the
trajectory from integrated-LR statistics (cumulative LR / intrinsic time / convolutional
functionals). FSL and its follow-ups also derive optimal schedules~\cite{li2025fsl}. All are
adiabatic in our sense, and our rate-dependent term is the leading correction they omit; it
is complementary to MPL's exponent $\gamma$ (which tunes the adiabatic kernel), not a
replacement for it.

\paragraph{Cooldown dynamics and schedule geometry.}
The river-valley picture~\cite{wen2024} explains qualitatively why decay ``reveals
progress'', and explicitly models the noise penalty as \emph{instantaneous} in the current
LR---the adiabatic assumption we refine. Dremov~et~al.~\cite{dremov2025} study the cooldown
empirically (including the role of AdamW's $\beta_2$ and the decay shape), framing it through a
bias--variance trade-off rather than a rate-dependent lag. Late-decay
analyses~\cite{kaplan2020} and schedule-shape searches~\cite{naganuma2026} study related
questions (why a long high-LR phase helps; what optimal shapes look like) but do not model the
loss's rate dependence.

\paragraph{SGD relaxation physics.}
That a quadratic mode relaxes with $\tau\sim1/(\eta\lambda)$ and that the SGD noise floor
scales with $\eta$ are classical, from noisy-quadratic and Langevin analyses of
SGD~\cite{zhang2019nqm}; adaptive methods inherit a preconditioned version~\cite{malladi2022},
operating at an adaptive edge of stability~\cite{cohen2021,cohen2022}. We do not claim these
ingredients as new. Our contribution is the synthesis: identifying that adiabatic loss-curve
laws miss exactly this relaxation lag, deriving the correction with a leading-order
amplitude identity, and building a parameter-free cross-scale predictor from it.

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:disc}
The gain is regime-specific (data-poor sharp decays); $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ and $c$ are
\emph{measured}, not computed from first principles (this needs the noise spectrum $\{s_i\}$,
which we do not have), and $\lambda_{\mathrm{slow}}$ is only \emph{approximately} scale-invariant
---it tracks the slowest preconditioned mode and drifts when the spectral \emph{shape} changes
(\S\ref{sec:repro2}), so the cross-scale transfer is a leading-order, same-architecture
approximation. The derivation also assumes a locally quadratic landscape with a static spectrum
and diagonal, uncorrelated noise---the model-level assumptions MPL itself rests on. Finally, the
quantitative $\tau\propto1/\eta$ relation, clean in the controlled simulation, is \emph{not}
resolved on the $\sim$10M transformer (\S\ref{sec:repro2}); confirming it on larger real models,
and characterising the precise relationship to $\gamma$, are the main open items.

\section{Conclusion}
Existing loss-curve laws are adiabatic approximations. We identified, derived from AdamW's
preconditioned dynamics, and validated the leading non-adiabatic correction: a rate-dependent
term whose relaxation time follows the classical $\tau\propto1/\eta$ ($p=0.84\pm0.17$,
confirmed for the loss-curve residual) and whose scale-invariant shape enables parameter-free
cross-scale prediction ($-44\%$ on held-out sharp decays). The loss curve depends not only on
how much LR has been spent, but on how fast it is being spent.
